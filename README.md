# Emergency Community Framework

This is a framework for bootstrapping a community — teaching a group of people to work together to meet each other's needs. It is intended for communities under stress: newly formed, economically devastated, or simply trying to build something more deliberate than what they inherited.

The whole thing rests on one idea: humans are innately valuable. A community where people are present has inherent value. That's not a starting assumption to be justified — it's the foundation everything else is built on.

## What This Is Not

**Not a social network.** This is not a platform for posting, following, liking, or building an audience. The goal is economic coordination and community governance, not engagement metrics. There is no feed, no profile to curate, no algorithmic amplification.

**Not a cryptocurrency project.** The community currency here is not a speculative asset. It does not appreciate by design. It is not traded on exchanges. Demurrage means it loses value over time — the opposite of an investment. It is a tool for coordinating labor and resources within a community, nothing more.

**Not a marketplace app.** This is not an interface for buying and selling between strangers at arm's length. It is infrastructure for a community that already has, or is building, real relationships. The economic layer is in service of those relationships, not a substitute for them.

**Not a charity platform.** The framework does not route donations or manage philanthropic giving. The floor — shelter, food, healthcare — is met through coordinated community production, not redistribution from outside.

**Not a replacement for government.** This framework does not claim to govern a jurisdiction, adjudicate disputes between people who haven't consented to its process, or replace public services. It operates within an existing legal context and is designed to complement, not supplant, whatever formal institutions a community interacts with.

**Not a utopian blueprint.** No assumptions are made about human nature improving. The design works with people as they are, by aligning incentives rather than demanding virtue. Communities using this framework will have conflicts, make mistakes, and sometimes fail. The framework tries to make recovery possible, not failure impossible.

**Not a finished product.** This is a framework under active development. The documentation describes intent and design. The software implements what it implements. There will be gaps between them.

## What This Is

**Free and open source.** The code is public. Anyone can run it, modify it, and adapt it to their community's needs. No license fees, no vendor lock-in, no company whose interests need to be aligned with yours. If the project is abandoned, the code remains.

**Decentralized.** There is no central server that communities depend on. Each community runs its own node. Nodes can federate — trading across community boundaries, backing each other up, coordinating on shared resources — but no single point controls the network. A community that loses its connection to other nodes continues to function internally.

**Able to run on a Raspberry Pi.** The infrastructure requirement is intentionally minimal. A $50 single-board computer, a cheap internet connection, and a little technical help is enough to run a node. This is not an accident — it is a design constraint. Infrastructure that requires expensive hardware or managed cloud services creates dependencies that a community under stress cannot afford.

**Something a community should outgrow.** This framework is for the bootstrap phase. A community that has successfully organized its economy, built its institutions, and developed the social capital to sustain itself will eventually find this software limiting. That is the goal. The measure of success is not adoption — it is the community's growing capacity to do things this software doesn't know how to do yet. The framework is scaffolding, not a permanent structure.

## Precedents

This is not a new idea. Communities have organized their own economic and social infrastructure before, and it has worked. This framework builds on three of those experiments.

From the mid-19th century through the early 20th, working-class communities across Europe and North America organized themselves into mutual aid societies — fraternal lodges, benefit societies, and cooperative associations. Members paid small regular dues; the society paid out when a member was sick, injured, or died. At their peak in the United States, tens of millions of people belonged to one. Immigrants built them along ethnic and occupational lines. The Black community built extensive networks as a direct response to systematic exclusion from white-controlled institutions. These organizations provided healthcare, life insurance, unemployment relief, and social support to populations that had no access to state welfare and couldn't afford private alternatives. They were not charities dispensing aid from above — they were communities governing their own welfare, pooling risk, and holding each other up.

When Argentina's economy collapsed in 2001, the peso became worthless overnight and the government froze bank accounts. Rather than wait for the formal system to recover, communities across the country created their own. Local networks issued *créditos* — a scrip currency people used to trade labor, food, and services with their neighbors. At the peak, millions of Argentinians were participating. The formal economy had failed them; they built something else.

M-Pesa, launched in Kenya in 2007, showed that a simple, accessible medium of exchange could unlock economic activity in communities the banking system had written off. No branch, no account, no collateral — just a phone. It didn't require the formal economy to work. It worked around it.

This framework tries to combine all three. The mutual aid society's model of collective welfare and shared governance. The *créditos*' demonstration that a community can issue its own currency and coordinate its own economy. M-Pesa's insistence that the infrastructure should be light, accessible, and available to people the formal system ignores. When the systems people depend on fail or exclude them, communities find a way. This is an attempt to help them find it faster.
