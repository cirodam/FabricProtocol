## I. What This Is

A protocol for human communities to organize mutual exchange and collective care. Not an app. Not an ideology. Not a political project. A set of principles and practices any community can implement with whatever tools they have — digital or paper.

The protocol is designed for people who have been left behind by the modern economy. People whose labor AI and automation have made redundant. Communities whose productive capacity the market can’t see because it isn’t profitable. The growing number of people who have looked at the deal on offer and decided it isn’t good enough.

### The Core Distinction

This protocol is **need-motivated** rather than **profit-motivated**. Production follows genuine human necessity. The farmer grows food because people need to eat, not because a commodity price makes it profitable. Every design decision flows from this distinction.

### What It Is Not

- **Not a social media platform.** No feeds, no engagement optimization, no attention harvesting.
- **Not a charity platform.** Reciprocal exchange, not one-directional giving.
- **Not a political project.** Need over ideology. Works across political difference.
- **Not a startup.** No investors, no extraction, no growth-for-its-own-sake.
- **Not designed for permanent dependency.** Built to be outgrown.
- **Not utopian.** Works with humans as they actually are.

The test for every feature: does this help people work together to meet each other’s needs? If yes it might belong. If no it doesn’t.

## II. The Monetary System

The monetary design is the most carefully reasoned element of the protocol. Every decision was made for specific reasons and the reasoning matters.

### A. The Fundamental Rules

**Rule 1**
Individual members never go below zero. Their balance is always zero or positive. There is no individual debt.

**Rule 2**
The commons fund is the only entity that can go negative. Its negative balance is the money supply. This is not a problem. It is the system working correctly.

**Rule 3**
The commons fund is the sole issuer of credits. Every credit in circulation was issued by the commons fund through a starting loan, a purchase commitment payment, or a direct loan to a member in need.

### B. How Credits Come Into Existence

Credits enter circulation exclusively through commons fund actions.

#### Starting Loans

Every new member receives a starting loan from the commons fund. This enables immediate participation from day one. The loan is denominated in the community’s chosen credit unit. The commons fund balance decreases by the loan amount. The member’s balance increases by the same amount.

The starting loan is not a gift. It is the community’s investment in the member’s participation. The member is expected to contribute enough over their membership to repay it through the levy mechanism. If they leave early the commons fund absorbs the unrecovered portion as the cost of community building.

The starting loan size is a governance decision. It should be large enough for genuine participation from day one. Small enough that the commons fund can absorb writeoffs without going deeply negative if a portion of new members leave early. Calibrated roughly to one month of typical community consumption.

#### Purchase Commitment Payments

The commons fund posts purchase commitments for goods and services the community collectively needs. When a member delivers what was committed to they receive credits from the commons fund. No new credits created speculatively. Credits issued in exchange for real goods delivered to the commons store.

This is the primary mechanism for coordinating production toward genuine community need. The commons governance assesses what the community needs. Posts a commitment at a price covering real production costs. Members with capacity respond. Production happens. Needs are met. The money supply expanded only because real productive output expanded with it.

#### Direct Loans for Hardship

The commons fund can loan credits directly to members facing genuine acute hardship. Approved through the governance process. No interest. Flexible repayment calibrated to what the member can actually contribute. Forgiveness available through governance decision for genuine inability to repay.

The loan is not charity. It is the community extending support to a member with the expectation of future contribution when circumstances improve. The social relationship matters as much as the credit transfer.

### C. How Credits Return to the Commons Fund

- **Transaction levy:** a small percentage of every exchange flows automatically to the commons fund. Invisible. Proportional to economic activity.
- **Demurrage:** large positive balances decay over time. The decay flows to the commons fund. Discourages hoarding. Encourages circulation.
- **Loan repayments:** credits returned by members who received commons fund loans.
- **Member exit returns:** positive balances returned to the commons fund when members depart formally.
- **Commons contributions:** members voluntarily contributing surplus goods credited from the commons fund return those credits through the levy on the recognition transaction.

### D. The Money Supply

Total credits in circulation equals the commons fund’s total negative balance. Always. By definition. Every credit that exists was issued by the commons fund. Every credit that returns reduces the commons fund’s negative balance.

The money supply scales naturally with community size because each new member receives a starting loan. More members means more credits in circulation. The supply breathes with the community’s actual size rather than being fixed arbitrarily or expanded by political will.

The money supply also scales with demonstrated trust. Members who have been in the community longer and have contributed more can access larger loans from the commons fund. Not assessed subjectively. Determined by transparent automatically-applied thresholds based on transaction history, commons contributions, governance participation, and time.

#### Why the Commons Fund Going Negative Is Normal

A commons fund at zero means no credits exist in the economy. Nobody has anything to spend. The economy is dead. A commons fund at negative 10,000 means 10,000 credits are circulating. Members are exchanging. Needs are being met. The negative balance is the money supply existing.

### E. Credit Value and the Unit Question

The community chooses what unit to denominate credits in. This is an open question still being developed for inter-community exchange. For intra-community use the unit needs to be something everyone understands and that is grounded in real productive capacity.

The leading candidates are kilowatt hours of energy (as a unit of work in the physics sense), calories of staple food, or a basket combining both. The kilowatt hour appeals because energy underlies all productive activity — everything humans produce is ultimately energy transformation. Calories appeal because food is the most fundamental human need and most legible to every community member.

The basket combining both is probably most robust. A credit redeemable for one kilowatt hour of electricity from the community energy store, or for a defined caloric quantity of staple food from the commons food store, or a proportional combination. Multiple physical floors rather than one. More stable because backed by the diversity of the community’s productive capacity.

This remains an open question. What is settled is that the unit should be grounded in something physically real that any member can verify. Not a government promise. Not collective belief. Something you can hold, eat, or use.

### F. Deflation Is Not the Enemy

When community productive capacity increases — when someone builds better growing infrastructure, develops more efficient tools, contributes knowledge that multiplies others’ productivity — the same credits buy more. Productivity deflation. This is the monetary system working correctly.

The conventional fear of deflation applies to debt-based consumer economies where falling prices cause debt burdens to increase and demand to collapse. Your community has no individual debt. Demurrage prevents hoarding. The deflationary spiral mechanism simply does not exist in this system.

Productivity deflation rewards past contribution. The credits you earned last year by contributing to the community buy more this year because the community has become more capable. That is the right signal. A system that inflates savings away punishes past contribution. A system with productivity deflation honors it.

### G. The Commons Fund as Democratic Central Bank

The commons fund performs the functions of a central bank. It manages the money supply through its lending and issuance decisions. But it is a central bank governed democratically by the community it serves rather than by unaccountable technocrats serving capital.

The Federal Reserve is captured by the financial system it was created to regulate. Your commons fund is governed by the people whose lives depend on its decisions. Same function. Completely different governance. The function is not the problem. The governance is. This protocol solves the governance problem by making the monetary authority identical to the community’s democratic process.

## III. The Commons Fund

The commons fund is the community’s material expression of its values. Every credit that flows in and every deployment decision is the community answering the question: what do we collectively owe each other? Modern capitalism answers that question with essentially nothing. Your community answers differently.

### A. What the Commons Fund Is

A collectively owned pool of credits that no individual owns and the community governs democratically. It is simultaneously the community’s central bank, its savings pool, its investment fund, its insurance mechanism, and its expression of collective values. All of these in one transparent democratically governed institution.

The commons fund’s negative balance is the money supply. Its positive reserve is the community’s collective savings. The governance layer manages both through the same democratic process.

### B. Inflows

- **Transaction levy:** small automatic percentage of every exchange. The community taxes its own economic activity to fund collective needs.
- **Demurrage:** large balance decay flowing to the commons. Hoarding funds the commons. The anti-hoarding mechanism funds collective goods.
- **Loan repayments:** credits returning from members who received commons fund loans.
- **Member exit returns:** unspent positive balances returning when members formally depart.
- **Direct contributions:** members who choose to contribute surplus credits beyond their levy obligation.

### C. Outflows

- **Starting loans:** credits issued to new members. The community’s investment in new participation.
- **Universal floor allocation:** ongoing credits ensuring every member’s basic needs are met. Unconditional. Constitutional.
- **Crisis response:** rapid deployment for acute member hardship. Pre-authorized up to a threshold without requiring a vote.
- **Purchase commitments:** credits paid to members delivering goods or services the community needs.
- **Community investment:** productive infrastructure, training, equipment, shared tools.
- **Commons maintenance:** compensating stewardship of shared assets that markets fail to value.
- **Direct hardship loans:** credits extended to members in genuine need with flexible repayment.

### D. Priority Order

When the commons fund is constrained not all outflows are equal. The priority order reflects values made operational.

1. **First:** universal floor allocation and acute crisis response. Constitutional. Cannot be cut without betraying what the community is.
2. **Second:** honoring outstanding loan commitments to members who were promised support.
3. **Third:** purchase commitments already made to members who have delivered goods in good faith.
4. **Fourth:** ongoing investment and maintenance spending, reduced proportionally if necessary.
5. **Last:** new investment commitments, paused until fund health is restored.

The community that cuts the floor first destroys the trust everything else depends on. The community that protects the floor last demonstrates that its values are real.

### E. Commons Fund Health

The commons fund balance is the most important community health indicator. Its trajectory tells the community more about its actual wellbeing than any other metric.

| State | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Growing | Active economy, good contribution culture, prudent deployment. Healthy. |
| Stable | Balanced inflows and outflows. Sustainable. |
| Slowly declining | Early warning. Governance review triggered. Not crisis yet but requires attention. |
| Consistently declining | Community is consuming more than it produces collectively. Requires genuine response. |

A consistently negative commons fund means the community’s productive capacity is insufficient to sustain its collective obligations. The response is not to abandon the obligations. It is to build the productive capacity the obligations require. Increase food production. Recruit productive members. Invest in tools that multiply capacity. Make the commitment sustainable by building what it demands.

### F. What a Negative Commons Fund Means

When the commons fund absorbs a negative balance writeoff — from a member who left early, from a member who died — the community is making a collective statement. We collectively extended credit to this person. We collectively absorb the cost of that credit not being fully repaid. The obligation was always ours collectively not any specific individual’s.

This makes the social contract explicit. When Maria went negative to receive something from John the community collectively stood behind her obligation. The commons fund absorbing the writeoff makes that implicit collective backing visible and honest. There is no mysterious creditor left holding an unpaid debt. The community extended the credit. The community absorbs the cost. Clean. Honest.

## IV. Universal Basic Needs

> Every member’s basic needs are met regardless of their productive capacity. This is not charity. It is what membership means.

### A. The Normal Distribution of Productive Capacity

Every community at every point in history has had members who can contribute less than they consume. Children. Elderly members. People with disabilities or chronic illness. People in temporary crisis. People in caregiving roles that reduce their market-visible contribution.

This is not an exception to be managed. It is the normal condition of human community. Every person alive was entirely non-productive for the first decade or more of their life. Most will be significantly less productive in their final years. Most will have periods in between where capacity is reduced.

The question is not whether the community cares for members who cannot currently contribute. The question is whether the community is strong enough to do so sustainably. That is the community’s challenge to meet. Not the vulnerable member’s burden to resolve.

### B. What Basic Needs Means

- **Calories:** enough food to sustain health and function. Not gourmet. Enough.
- **Shelter:** adequate protection from the elements. Not luxurious. Adequate.
- **Medical care:** access to the community’s health capacity when genuinely needed.
- **Social connection:** genuine belonging and real relationship with other community members.
- **Safety:** protection from violence and exploitation within the community.
- **Dignity:** treatment as a full member with standing, not as a dependent or charity recipient.

Everything above this floor operates through individual productive capacity and marketplace exchange. The floor is the unconditional commitment. Everything above it is earned through contribution and exchange freely entered into.

### C. Sustainability

Universal basic needs provision is sustainable when the community’s productive capacity exceeds its total consumption needs by sufficient margin to fund the floor for all members. The census and needs tracking tell the community whether this condition is met.

The historical evidence is clear. Hunter-gatherer bands redistributed to all members for 290,000 years. Hutterite communities have run fully redistributive systems since the 1500s and are consistently more productive per capita than surrounding communities. The Mondragon cooperatives have operated profitably in competitive global markets for 70 years with explicit redistribution. The redistribution does not reduce productivity. Genuine community belonging increases it.

Redistribution is legitimate when productive members can see where their contribution goes, understand the reciprocal nature of the relationship across time, see genuine need rather than preference being met, have their own contributions genuinely recognized, and participate democratically in governance of the floor. This protocol embeds all of these conditions.

### D. Non-Market Contributions

A member who cannot do physical labor or generate marketplace credits may still contribute genuine value that the monetary system cannot easily see.

- **Presence and attention:** being available to children, elderly members, or anyone who needs company.
- **Institutional memory:** knowing the community’s history and helping newer members navigate it.
- **Peer support:** being present with members in difficulty. Listening without judgment.
- **Knowledge contribution:** teaching whatever they know regardless of physical capacity.
- **Governance participation:** bringing perspectives that able-bodied productive members lack.

The commons fund can explicitly recognize these contributions through direct recognition credits separate from marketplace exchange. The community decides what non-market contributions are worth recognizing and at what rate. This is the monetary system seeing value that markets systematically miss.

## V. Community Census and Needs Tracking

A community that knows itself can govern itself. The census and needs tracking are the community turning its attention on itself honestly. Not for external reporting. For its own self-knowledge and planning.

### A. The Census

A continuously updated picture of community reality. Self-reported by members. Not surveillance. Members contribute their information because they understand it serves the community they belong to.

#### People

- Every member: age range, health status, productive capacity, primary skills, current needs.
- Household composition: who lives together, what shared resources they have.
- Members requiring significant commons support: nature and estimated duration of need.
- Skills inventory: everything members can offer. Professional credentials and practical capabilities both.

#### Productive Assets

The census records what exists in the community. Individual ownership is unaffected. A member who owns a tractor still owns it. The census makes the tractor visible so the community knows it exists and can ask about it. Visibility without obligation.

- **Land:** who has it, how much, current use, productive potential.
- **Tools and equipment:** power tools, vehicles, processing equipment, medical equipment.
- **Infrastructure:** structures, storage capacity, water systems, energy generation.
- **Knowledge assets:** documented techniques, reference materials, specialized expertise.

#### Voluntary Contribution Layer

Separate from the census, members indicate what they’re willing to make available. The census creates visibility. This layer records intent. A member lists their tractor as available for loan at a credit rate, as a monthly commons contribution, or not currently available. All three are legitimate. The census creates no obligation. It creates the possibility of conversation.

### B. Needs Tracking

Most economic systems track production and exchange. Your community also tracks genuine need. This is radical. Markets track purchasing power not need. The family that needs food but has no money is economically invisible. Your community sees what markets cannot because it knows its members.

#### The Caloric Foundation

Calories are the most fundamental unit of community survival. Track this first.

- **Total caloric need monthly:** member count times average daily caloric requirement adjusted for age and activity. Roughly 2,000 calories per person per day as a baseline.
- **Current caloric production and storage:** what gardens produce, what the commons store holds.
- **The caloric security ratio:** production and storage divided by need. The most important number the community monitors.
- **Trend over time:** improving, stable, or declining.

A community of 500 people needs roughly 30 million calories per month. Knowing this and comparing it to current production is the foundation of food security planning. The gap between need and supply is the work to be done.

#### Other Needs Categories

- **Medical:** chronic conditions, medication requirements, current capacity versus need.
- **Shelter:** members adequately housed, maintenance requirements, new capacity needed.
- **Tools and equipment:** productive activities constrained by lack of tools.
- **Knowledge gaps:** skills the community lacks that it genuinely needs.
- **Social and mental health:** members in genuine distress, peer support capacity versus need.

### C. The Needs Dashboard

The census and needs tracking produce a community health dashboard that makes the gap between what the community needs and what it has visible at all times to all members.

- Caloric security ratio and trend.
- Medical capacity versus identified need.
- Housing adequacy percentage.
- Commons fund balance and trajectory.
- Active productive members versus members requiring significant commons support, with trend.
- Key asset gaps identified by census.
- Natural systems health indicators: soil, water, biodiversity trends.

This dashboard is the community looking honestly at itself. Not performing health for outside observers. Genuinely assessing whether it can care for its members. The governance layer reviews it regularly and makes investment decisions based on what it shows.

### D. Needs-Driven Investment

The needs gap drives commons fund investment. The food security gap says the community needs more caloric production. The commons fund posts purchase commitments for calorie-dense crops. Members with land and growing capacity respond. Production increases to meet identified need.

Every commons fund investment is traceable to a specific identified need. Not to political priority. Not to lobbying. To the community’s honest assessment of what its members require. This is the difference between need-motivated economics and profit-motivated economics expressed as a governance practice.

## VI. Marketplace and Directory

The marketplace and directory are where the monetary system meets real productive activity. Where needs connect to capacity. Where the abstract credit system becomes concrete exchange between specific people.

### A. The Directory

A living map of community capacity. The answer to two questions for every member: what can I offer and what do I need. Makes invisible productive capacity visible. The foundation the marketplace builds on.

- **Skills, goods, services, time, knowledge:** anything with genuine use value to community members.
- **Transaction history and contribution record:** visible reputation built through actual participation. Not a score. A record.
- **Vouching chains:** who sponsored whom, making trust networks legible.
- **Asset visibility:** what productive tools and equipment exist in the community, with owner-specified availability.

The directory solves a problem that the market cannot. Most communities have enormous latent productive capacity that nobody knows about. The retired mechanic. The trained nurse who isn’t currently working. The person with a truck who would help haul things. The directory makes this visible and activates it.

### B. The Marketplace

Facilitates exchange by matching needs with capacity. Functional. The goal is to get people to the real-world interaction as quickly as possible and then get out of the way.

#### Private Transactions

Bilateral exchange at negotiated credit rates. Maria offers eggs. John needs eggs. They agree on a price. Credits transfer. Transaction recorded in the ledger. Standard marketplace operation.

#### Group Offerings

Skill shares, classes, and workshops posted with date, maximum participants, and credit cost per person. Confirmed when minimum attendance is reached. The app coordinates. The gathering happens in person. The knowledge transfers between humans in physical space.

#### Commons Purchase Commitments

The most important marketplace mechanism. The commons governance identifies a gap in what the community needs versus what it produces. Posts a purchase commitment: *We will buy 100 pounds of wheat at X credits per pound by this date.*

Members with capacity respond. Credits flow from the commons fund to producers when goods are delivered. Production is coordinated toward genuine community need without central planning. The monetary system directs productive activity toward what the community actually requires. No crop is ever destroyed because the price is too low. The community pre-commits to buying at prices that cover production costs because the community genuinely needs the food.

#### Commons Contributions

Surplus goods offered to the community collectively rather than sold to specific buyers. The member brings abundant garden produce to the commons store. The commons fund recognizes the contribution with credits assessed at honest productive value. The goods become available to members who need them, prioritized by need rather than by purchasing power.

The recognition is modest. The contribution is permanent. Your name is on the record. The community saw what you gave. That visibility does more for contribution motivation than any credit payment.

### C. Exchange Principles

- No star ratings or scoring systems. These create anxiety and gaming. Transaction notes build reputation naturally through accumulated history.
- Listings expire automatically. The marketplace stays current and action-oriented.
- Prices emerge from negotiation informed by visible market history. Standard rates develop through accumulated custom not imposed by governance.
- The baker who wants to expand works with a cooperative partner, not an employee. Surplus is shared proportionally to contribution. Nobody extracts from anyone else’s labor.
- The marketplace facilitates exchange and gets out of the way. Every interaction should push toward physical reality not substitute for it.

### D. The Cooperative as the Natural Production Unit

When members want to combine productive capacity they form cooperatives rather than employer-employee relationships. The distinction matters.

**Wage labor:** one person directs another’s work and captures the surplus. The worker is subordinated. Their relationship to the product of their work is severed. This produces the alienation that capitalism has normalized.

**Cooperative:** members combine their capacity, share the work, and share the surplus proportionally to contribution. Everyone has genuine stake. The relationship to the product is direct. This is the natural production unit for communities built on mutual aid.

People coming from a wage labor background will instinctively try to hire employees. The protocol addresses this not through prohibition but through making cooperative structure easy. Template agreements. Guidance for common decisions. Community members with cooperative experience available as resources. The path of least resistance should be the cooperative path.

## VII. Governance

Democratic governance with constitutional protections. Every significant decision made through community process. The values are embedded in the design not dependent on the intentions of founders.

### A. Core Mechanisms

- **Proposals:** any member can put something to the community. Clear format — what’s proposed, what problem it solves, what it costs, who’s accountable. Visible to all members. Deliberation period before vote.
- **Graduated thresholds:** simple majority for routine decisions, supermajority for governance changes, near-consensus for constitutional changes.
- **Elected functional roles with term limits:** commons fund steward, dispute facilitator, census keeper, marketplace steward. Not status positions. Specific accountable functions.
- **Transparency log:** every decision recorded with its reasoning. The community’s governance history is its institutional memory.
- **Restorative conflict resolution:** the goal is restored relationship and continued cooperation, not punishment.

### B. Constitutional Protections

Some things cannot be voted away. Protected at the constitutional level. Require near-consensus to change even then.

- **Universal floor allocation:** every member receives basic needs support regardless of balance or contribution. Unconditional.
- **Unconditional membership:** belonging is not contingent on productive capacity.
- **Data portability and exit rights:** members can always take their data and leave. No lock-in.
- **Transparent ledger:** the commons fund and all credit flows are visible to all members at all times.
- **Democratic minimum:** governance cannot be captured by any individual or small group. Mechanisms for recall and accountability are permanent.

### C. The Vouching System

New members join through existing members who sponsor them. The sponsor stakes their reputation on the new member’s genuine participation. This imports existing trust relationships into the system rather than trying to generate trust from scratch.

Vouching is not bureaucratic gatekeeping. It is the community growing through genuine relationship rather than anonymous open enrollment. The quality of community membership depends on the quality of the relationships through which members join.

### D. Credit Access and Trust

Starting loan: every member receives the same starting loan. The community’s equal investment in every new member’s participation.

Extended credit access grows with demonstrated engagement. Not assessed subjectively. Determined by transparent thresholds based on transaction history, commons contributions, governance participation, and time in the community.

| Tier | Criteria | Access |
|---|---|---|
| New member | Just joined | Starting loan. The community is getting to know you. |
| Established member | 6 months, 50+ transactions | Expanded loan access. Demonstrated contribution. |
| Long-standing member | 2+ years, 200+ transactions | Maximum loan access. Deep community investment. |

This ties money supply expansion to demonstrated trustworthiness. A more active community has more credits in circulation because its members have built more genuine relationships. The monetary system is honest about the community’s actual trust level.

## VIII. Natural Systems

The natural world doesn’t get a seat at the table in the current system because the current system treats it as a free input. Your communities explicitly reject this. The natural systems your community depends on are not externalities to be managed. They are the foundation everything else rests on.

### A. The Commons Fund for Natural Systems

A portion of every transaction levy and demurrage flow goes specifically to natural systems stewardship. This fund compensates members who do the work of ecological care. Maintaining riparian buffers. Tending pollinator habitat. Monitoring soil health. Maintaining the seed bank.

These are currently uncompensated activities because their value flows to the whole community and the natural system rather than to any specific paying customer. The natural systems fund pays for ecosystem services the market has always taken for free.

### B. The Natural Systems Census

Extend the community census to document natural assets alongside human ones.

- **Watershed health:** current condition, trend, what it requires to remain healthy.
- **Soil health:** organic matter content, microbial health, water holding capacity, trend.
- **Local ecosystem:** species diversity, pollinator populations, indicators of ecological health.
- **Energy resources:** solar potential, wind regime, water flow for community generation capacity.

The natural systems census makes visible what is currently invisible to every economic decision-making process. You cannot manage what you cannot see. The trend data is most important. A declining watershed is an early warning. Improving soil organic matter is evidence that regenerative practices are working.

### C. The Seven Generations Frame

Every major governance decision evaluated against its impact seven generations forward. Not just next year. Not just our lifetime. This is not mysticism. It is appropriate accounting for the time horizons of the natural systems the community depends on.

Soil takes decades to build and years to destroy. Forests take centuries to mature. Aquifers take millennia to fill. A governance layer that accounts for these time horizons makes decisions that quarterly-profit-focused institutions cannot. The seven generations frame is the appropriate time horizon for managing systems that operate on those timescales.

### D. Aligned Incentives

The most fundamental ecological protection in your community is not regulation. It is genuine dependency on local natural systems.

Your food comes from your watershed. Your water comes from your aquifer. Your energy comes from your sun and wind. The health of those systems is directly your health. When your survival depends on your local ecosystem’s health you have every reason to maintain it. No external regulation required. The dependency is the relationship. The relationship is the protection.

## IX. Community Lifecycle

Communities are living systems. They form, grow, split, shrink, and die. None of this requires permission or represents failure. The protocol provides tools for every stage of this lifecycle.

### A. Formation

A community forms when enough people have enough need and enough trust to start. The founding group doesn’t need to be large. Twenty to thirty people with genuine relationships and genuine motivation is enough to begin. The directory gets populated. The first commons fund contributions establish the starting pool. The first exchanges begin building transaction history. The community is alive.

The cold start problem — a mutual credit system with three members isn’t useful — is solved by the starting loan. Every member has something to spend from day one. The marketplace has participants. Exchange can begin before any member has earned credits through contribution.

### B. Growth and Fission

Communities grow through genuine relationship and vouching. Not through marketing. Not through open enrollment. Each new member arrives through an existing member who sponsors them.

When a community grows to the point where intimacy degrades — where governance requires bureaucracy rather than relationship, where not everyone knows everyone well enough for social trust to govern behavior — it splits. Not as failure. As healthy reproduction.

The daughter community launches with a positive credit line from the parent and a federation relationship already established. The parent community doesn’t lose those members as a resource. They become a federated neighbor. The split is planned before it’s needed, during stable times when everyone is cooperative, not during the stress that would make splitting contentious.

### C. Death and Dissolution

Some communities will not survive. Demographic collapse. Climate stress eliminating productive capacity. Loss of the economic foundation that made the community viable. These are real and sometimes unavoidable.

The protocol ensures that community death is graceful rather than chaotic. Members receive guaranteed consideration in federated communities. Their reputation and contribution history travel with them. Commons assets distribute to members or transfer to neighboring communities. The seed bank and agricultural knowledge transfer to the federation knowledge commons. The community’s learning survives even when the community doesn’t.

A community that completes its purpose and dissolves has succeeded. Permanence is a bureaucratic value not a community value. The relationships formed within a community persist after its formal dissolution. The people carry the protocol with them.

## X. Anti-Exploitation Design

The modern internet and modern economy were deliberately engineered to exploit. Every design choice in this protocol goes the other way.

- **No advertising.** Ever. Structurally impossible, not just prohibited.
- **No data monetization.** Community data belongs to the community.
- **No engagement optimization.** Intent-driven navigation. You come with a purpose, accomplish it, leave.
- **No variable reward mechanics.** No likes, no follower counts, no public approval metrics.
- **No infinite scroll.** Lists have bottoms. The interface tells you when you’re done.
- **No notifications designed to create anxiety.** Only genuine signals requiring response.
- **Open source with copyleft (AGPL).** Inspectable. Modifications returned to the commons.
- **Self-hosted.** No central server to capture, shut down, or monetize.

### The Test

Is this feature helping people work together to meet each other’s needs? Or is it creating dependency on the platform? Dependency doesn’t belong regardless of how useful it seems.

## XI. The Paper Version

The protocol is not the software. The software is one implementation. A community running on paper ledgers, physical bulletin boards, and weekly market days is running the same protocol. The paper version is not degraded. In some ways it is more honest. The physical ledger that lives at the community center is more transparent than a database that requires technical knowledge to audit.

- **Credit ledger:** a physical book recording every transaction and every member’s balance. Visible to anyone who asks.
- **Commons fund ledger:** a separate book recording every inflow and outflow with governance authorization.
- **Directory:** a posted list of member offerings and needs. Updated regularly at the community center.
- **Marketplace bulletin board:** listings posted physically. Expire after a set date.
- **Weekly market day:** the primary exchange mechanism. People bring what they have and need. Exchanges happen in person.
- **Town meeting:** governance decisions made in person. Recorded in the governance log.
- **Census document:** maintained and updated. Posted for community visibility.

The digital app coordinates and accelerates what the paper system does. It does not replace the physical gathering that is the actual community. Every digital feature should ask: does this facilitate a real-world interaction or substitute for one? Substitution is failure.

## XII. What Success Looks Like

Not metrics. Not growth. Not engagement. Not funding raised.

- The elderly member who doesn’t go hungry because her community knows she needs food.
- The family that keeps their home because the community helped when the income disappeared.
- The child who grows up knowing they belong to something larger than their household.
- The person who discovers they have something valuable to offer that the market declared worthless.
- The neighbor who turns out to be a full human being rather than the political abstraction they’d been told to fear.
- The baker who cooperates with a partner rather than employing them, and both are better for it.
- The farmer whose crop is never destroyed because the community pre-committed to buying it.
- The community that barely uses the app anymore because they don’t need it. That means it worked.

The deepest success is a community where nobody asks whether they can afford to care for their vulnerable members because the question has become incoherent. Of course we care for them. They are one of us. That is what *one of us* means.

## Open Questions

These are deliberately left open. Not failures of design. Areas where more thinking is needed or where communities should decide for themselves.

- **The credit unit:** kilowatt hours of energy, calories of food, a basket of both, or something else. The unit should be physically real and legible to all members. The specific unit is not yet settled.
- **Inter-community exchange:** how credits transfer between communities, what exchange rate mechanism is honest and low-friction. Left open deliberately. Inter-community design continues.
- **The exact starting loan amount:** calibrated to one month of typical community consumption as a guideline. Specific communities set their own based on commons fund health and local conditions.
- **Demurrage rate:** enough to discourage hoarding without creating excessive anxiety. Communities set their own. Governance adjusts based on observed behavior.
- **Transaction levy rate:** small enough to be invisible on any individual transaction. Large enough to fund the commons fund’s obligations. Communities set their own.
- **Cooperative formation templates:** the specific legal and governance templates that make cooperative formation easy. Need development.
- **Natural systems assessment methodology:** how exactly the community measures soil health, watershed health, ecosystem indicators. Needs specific development for each geographic context.
