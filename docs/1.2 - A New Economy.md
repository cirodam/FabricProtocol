# 1.2 A New Economy

## Presence

The first move of this framework is to recognize something the formal economy ignores: simply being present in a community has value.

Not productive presence. Not measurable contribution. Just being here — showing up, participating, aging in place, raising children, caring for neighbors, being part of the fabric of a place. A community of people who are committed to each other has something real, even if no market will price it.

This reframing matters because it changes who counts. In the formal economy, someone who can't work is a burden. A child, an elder, someone ill or recovering, a caregiver — these people are invisible to a system that measures value in output. In this framework, they are full members of the community, accumulating the same fundamental unit as everyone else.

That unit is the person-year.

---

## The Person-Year

A person-year is one year spent alive on this planet. It is the anchor for everything monetary in this system.

It measures time and presence, not productivity. A farmer and a retired teacher accumulate person-years at the same rate. An infant and a doctor accumulate them at the same rate. This is not an oversight — it is the point. The system is anchored to something demographic and egalitarian, something that cannot be gamed by skill or capital.

A community cannot fake a decade of membership. Person-years are slow-moving by design. An older, larger community has more accumulated person-years and a proportionally larger money supply — reflecting real accumulated social capacity: trust, infrastructure, institutional knowledge, demonstrated commitment to each other.

---

## Kin

The community currency is the **kin**. One kin is defined as 1/10,000 of a person-year of presence. Every member receives a continuous kin issuance from the community simply for being a member — unconditionally, automatically, as a recognition that their presence has value. This is not a wage. It is not a welfare payment. It is the community's monetary expression of the founding premise.

The kin exists only within the community that issues it. It cannot leave. This is not a technical limitation — it is the structural guarantee that the local economy cannot be drained by outside actors. The money supply grows as the community grows and ages. It shrinks when members leave, and the community collectively absorbs that cost through a small temporary tax called demurrage.

---

## Why a Community Mints Its Own Currency

A national currency is not neutral. It is issued by banks when they make loans, which means it flows toward creditworthy borrowers — businesses with collateral, individuals with income, projects that promise a return. A struggling community has none of these things in abundance. It is not creditworthy by the standards of the formal system. So the formal system's money does not flow there, or flows in and immediately flows back out to wherever returns are higher.

A community that waits for national currency to solve its problems is waiting for a system that has already decided it isn't worth investing in.

The alternative is to stop waiting. A community has real resources: people, land, skills, time, mutual obligation. What it lacks is a medium of exchange — a way to coordinate the flow of those resources between members. A community currency is that medium. It is not a replacement for national currency in every context. It is a tool for activating economic activity that the formal system leaves dormant.

This is not radical. It is what the Argentine community networks did with créditos. It is what hundreds of local currency experiments have done in various forms. When official money stops working for a community, the community makes its own.

The question is how to do it well — how to issue it fairly, anchor it to something real, and prevent it from being gamed or captured. That is what the central bank is for.

---

## Demurrage

The kin money supply expands as members join and age. It needs a mechanism to contract when it should — and to fund collective needs without requiring taxes on income or labor.

That mechanism is demurrage: a slow, periodic decay applied to kin balances above a basic threshold.

Demurrage is not a punishment. It is a structural feature with two functions.

The first is monetary. When a member leaves the community, the kin that was issued to them over their years of membership has already circulated — it now sits in other members' accounts. The community's central bank carries a deficit. Demurrage runs across all balances until that deficit is recovered and the money supply returns to where it should be. It stops automatically when the books are balanced. A stable community with no recent departures doesn't experience this.

The second is communal. The kin collected through demurrage on large balances flows into a community commons fund — a pool available to fund things the community collectively values: infrastructure, public services, care work, emergency support, whatever the community decides. This gives the currency a built-in mechanism for collective investment that does not depend on coercive taxation or voluntary charity.

Demurrage also discourages hoarding. A currency that decays slowly when held in large quantities is a currency that wants to circulate — wants to be spent on local goods, local labor, local services. This keeps the economy active and prevents the structural wealth concentration that eventually hollows out every community it takes hold in.

## The Beauty of it

What makes this system unusual is what it says about people.

In the formal economy, poverty is implicitly a personal failing. If you cannot find work, you are surplus. If your community is declining, it wasn't competitive enough. The market has spoken. The logic is relentless: value is produced, and if you aren't producing it, you aren't generating it. A child contributes nothing. An elder contributes nothing. A person recovering from illness, a parent raising children, someone whose skills the market no longer wants — all of them are, in economic terms, burdens.

This is not a description of reality. It is an artifact of how we've chosen to measure value.

A currency anchored to human existence says something different. It says that a person alive in a community — breathing, present, part of the social fabric — is already generating something real. Not metaphorically. Monetarily. Their presence is the backing for the currency. Their years are the anchor of the money supply. The community is wealthier for having them than it would be without them, and the accounting reflects that directly.
