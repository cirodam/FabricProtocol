# Qualified Sortition

## Why Americans Should Like This

Most Americans have stopped trusting elected officials — and for good reason. Elections select for people who are good at getting elected: fundraising, self-promotion, messaging, and making promises they don't intend to keep. Those skills have almost nothing to do with governing well. The result is a political class that is largely self-serving, beholden to donors, and insulated from the consequences of their own decisions.

Sortition removes that selection pressure entirely. You can't campaign for a draw. You can't buy your way onto a panel. You can't build a political career out of it. The people who end up making decisions are people who were living their lives, doing their work, and got called — the same way jurors get called.

Americans already accept this model instinctively. When a jury returns a verdict, people generally trust it — not because jurors are experts, but because they were randomly selected, had no stake in the outcome, and were required to deliberate. That trust is rarely extended to Congress.

Qualified sortition takes the jury model and adds the one thing juries sometimes lack: domain knowledge. Farming decisions are made by farmers. Healthcare decisions are made by healthcare workers. The people deciding are both knowledgeable and randomly selected — no career politicians, no donor class, no permanent experts who've captured the process.

---

## The Problem with Pure Voting

When a community votes on domain-specific matters — farming schedules, healthcare protocols, construction standards — the outcome is determined by whoever shows up and however they feel that day. People with no knowledge of farming vote on crop rotation. People with no experience in construction vote on building materials. The result is decisions that feel democratic but are epistemically weak.

Pure technocracy solves the knowledge problem but creates a different one: experts capture the domain. The same people make every decision, entrench their preferences, and insulate themselves from accountability. Expertise becomes a credential for permanent authority.

Qualified sortition is a third path.

---

## How It Works

Each functional domain maintains a **qualified pool** — a roster of members who meet defined criteria for participation in domain governance. The criteria are set and maintained by the community through the normal proposal process.

When a domain-level decision needs to be made, a **panel** is drawn randomly from the pool. The panel deliberates and decides. When their term or the specific decision is complete, they rotate back into the pool.

The key properties:
- **Qualified**: only members with demonstrated knowledge and stake participate
- **Random**: no one can campaign, accumulate power, or be selected through political influence
- **Rotating**: no permanent governing class forms within a domain

---

## Pool Qualification

Qualification criteria are domain-specific and community-defined. Examples:

- **Agriculture**: has completed a growing season as a working farmer, or holds a recognized agricultural training credential from the community
- **Healthcare**: holds an active role in the healthcare domain
- **Construction**: has completed supervised work hours in the relevant trade

Criteria should have two tiers where appropriate:
1. **Entry criteria** — what gets a member added to the pool
2. **Maintenance criteria** — what keeps them in it (ongoing participation, continuing practice)

Members can qualify for multiple domain pools. A nurse who also farms belongs to both.

---

## Panel Selection

When a decision requires a panel:

1. The domain requests a draw from the commonwealth's governance service
2. A panel of defined size is drawn randomly from the qualified pool
3. If the pool is smaller than the minimum panel size, the domain must flag this as a governance concern — pools that are too small indicate that qualification criteria may be too restrictive, or that domain participation needs to be expanded
4. The panel is convened, briefed on the matter, and given time to deliberate
5. Decision is recorded and attributed to the panel (not to individuals, preserving some anonymity)

---

## Weighting

Flat random selection is the default. Weighted selection — giving higher draw probability to more experienced members — is an option but should be used cautiously. Weighting reintroduces the tendency toward a permanent expert class and should require explicit community justification.

A reasonable middle ground: maintain experience tiers within the pool (e.g. apprentice, journeyman, senior) and require panels to include at least one senior member, while filling the rest by flat random draw.

---

## Term Limits and Cooldowns

A member who has just served on a panel should have a cooldown period before they can be drawn again. This prevents the same small subset of the pool from being repeatedly selected by chance clustering.

The cooldown length is a community statute. A reasonable default is: ineligible for re-draw until at least half the pool has been drawn once.

---

## Who Governs the Criteria

The qualification criteria for each domain's pool are set by **community statute**, not by the domain itself. This prevents a domain from setting its own entry bar so high that it becomes self-perpetuating, or so low that it becomes meaningless.

The process for changing pool criteria is the same as for any domain statute: proposal, public comment, vote by the domain's governing council (itself selected by sortition), subject to community veto.

---

## Relationship to the Commonwealth

The commonwealth maintains the pool registry and manages the draw mechanism. Draws are transparent and auditable — the randomness source is publicly verifiable, the pool membership is a matter of record, and draw results are logged.

This makes the commonwealth a neutral infrastructure provider for domain governance rather than a decision-maker in domain matters. It holds the process; the qualified pool holds the judgment.
