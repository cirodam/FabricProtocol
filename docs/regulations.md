# Regulations and Community Standards

## The Weight of the Regulatory State

The United States has accumulated hundreds of thousands of pages of federal, state, and local regulations. The original intent of most of them was legitimate — to protect people from harm, deception, or exploitation. But the system has metastasized into something that primarily serves incumbents, generates revenue for enforcement agencies, and creates liability shields for bureaucracies. The people it was meant to protect are often buried under compliance costs and procedural requirements that have nothing to do with safety.

One of the genuine freedoms of building a community outside that system is the ability to start over with a cleaner question: *what rules actually protect people, and what rules exist for other reasons?*

---

## Why So Many Regulations Exist

Most regulations exist because large, anonymous markets create information problems. When you buy meat from a stranger in a grocery store, you have no way of knowing how it was handled, who processed it, or whether the facility was clean. When a contractor builds your house, you can't verify their competence before the fact. Regulations substitute for the trust and accountability that exist naturally in small communities.

In a community where everyone knows who grows the food, who built the houses, and who is accountable for what — many of those information problems are solved by social structure rather than bureaucratic oversight. The farmer's reputation is on the line with every harvest. The builder lives in the community they build. Accountability is personal and immediate.

This doesn't eliminate the need for standards. It changes how they're set and enforced.

---

## What's Worth Keeping

These categories of protection exist because real people were seriously harmed before they were established. They represent genuine knowledge about what goes wrong when left unaddressed.

### Food Safety
Contamination, adulteration, and mislabeling are real risks. The pre-regulatory era saw deliberate adulteration of food for profit, outbreaks of foodborne illness at scale, and no recourse for consumers. A community needs clear standards for food handling, storage, and preparation — particularly for food produced for communal consumption rather than personal use.

The community's food domain sets and maintains these standards. They are enforced through domain accountability, not external inspectors.

### Structural Safety
Buildings have load requirements, fire egress requirements, and electrical safety requirements that are grounded in physics and fire behavior. These don't change because the community is small. A floor that can't support its load fails regardless of how much everyone trusts the builder.

The construction domain maintains structural standards. New construction is reviewed by domain members with relevant expertise — a functional equivalent of building inspection, run internally.

### Sanitation and Water Quality
Waterborne illness — cholera, typhoid, giardia — is not a historical curiosity. It returns whenever sanitation infrastructure fails or water sources are contaminated. The community maintains clear standards for water treatment, waste handling, and sanitation infrastructure.

### Workplace Safety
Fall protection, machinery guarding, electrical lockout, and hazardous materials handling exist because the failure modes are severe and often irreversible. These are not bureaucratic formalities — they are distilled from patterns of injury and death. The community adopts these standards in any domain that involves physical risk.

### Child Protection
Children cannot consent to labor, to hazardous conditions, or to exploitation. These protections are absolute and not subject to community variation.

## What Gets Replaced by Community Accountability

In a small community with transparent records and domain accountability, many regulatory functions are handled more effectively by social structure than by rule enforcement.

**Occupational licensing** in most forms can be dropped. The state licenses hairdressers, florists, interior designers, and dozens of other occupations primarily to protect incumbent practitioners from competition. A community decides who it trusts to do what based on demonstrated competence and track record — not credentials issued by a state board.

For higher-stakes domains (healthcare, construction, food production), the community maintains its own competency records and qualification criteria, managed through the qualified sortition pool system. These are community-defined, not state-imposed.

**Zoning and land use** as currently practiced is a byzantine system that primarily serves property owners who want to exclude others and protect their property values. A community makes land use decisions collectively through its governance process. The question isn't which bureaucratic zone a parcel falls in — it's what the community has decided about how land is used and for whose benefit.

**Procedural compliance theater** — the large category of rules that exist to protect agencies from legal liability rather than people from harm — is simply dropped. Documentation requirements, reporting requirements, and administrative processes are designed to serve the community's actual needs, not to generate paper trails for regulators.

---

## How Community Standards Are Made

Community standards replace regulations, but they are not informal or arbitrary. They follow the governance process:

1. A domain identifies a need for a standard (food handling temperatures, load-bearing requirements, water testing frequency, etc.)
2. A qualified sortition panel from that domain drafts the proposed standard
3. The proposal goes through public comment — any community member can raise concerns
4. The domain council votes to adopt, modify, or reject
5. The standard is recorded in the community's statute registry with a sunset date
6. It must be reviewed and affirmatively renewed or it expires

This process is slower than a bureaucrat issuing guidance, but it produces standards that the community actually understands, has consented to, and will actually follow — rather than rules that exist on paper and are gamed in practice.

---

## Interaction with External Law

The community does not operate in a legal vacuum. Members are still subject to applicable federal, state, and local law. The community's internal standards do not override external legal requirements — they supplement them where the external requirements are insufficient, and they replace internal bureaucracy where the community has the autonomy to govern itself.

The goal is not to be a sovereign territory outside the law. It is to build a community culture and internal governance that makes most external regulatory intervention unnecessary — because the community is already doing the right thing, transparently, with genuine accountability.
