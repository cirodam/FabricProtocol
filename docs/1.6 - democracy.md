# 1.6 Self-Governance

A community that depends on external authorities to make decisions about its own life is not fully a community — it is a constituency. It can petition. It can vote for representatives who may or may not act on its behalf. It cannot govern.

This framework is built on the assumption that a community should govern itself. The people who live with the consequences of decisions should be the people who make them. This is not a radical position. It is the original meaning of democracy, before democracy came to mean choosing between candidates every few years and then stepping aside.

---

## The Problem with Representatives

Representative democracy solved a real problem: direct participation is impractical at the scale of a nation-state. You cannot hold a town meeting for three hundred million people. So the compromise was representation — elect people to act on your behalf, hold them accountable at the next election.

The compromise has costs. Representatives accumulate power and interests of their own. The people best positioned to win elections are not ordinary members of the community — they are people with access to money, media, and the appetite for a particular kind of performance. The incentives of elected office reward people who are good at getting elected, which is not the same as being good at governing. Over time, the representative class becomes a distinct interest group, oriented toward its own reproduction.

The result is familiar: a political system in which ordinary people have limited influence over decisions that directly affect their lives, and in which the people who do make those decisions are systematically unrepresentative of the communities they nominally serve.

At the scale of a local community, the justification for this compromise disappears. The community is small enough for direct participation. There is no logistical barrier to everyone having a voice. The representative model is not a necessity at this scale — it is a habit imported from a context where it made more sense.

This framework has no politicians. There are no elected officials accumulating power between votes. There are no campaigns. There are roles — coordinators, officers, council members — but they are bounded, term-limited, accountable, and in some cases filled by sortition rather than election. The community governs through direct participation in decisions that affect it.

---

## Participatory Budgeting

The most concrete expression of self-governance is control over resources. A community that cannot decide how its shared resources are spent is not governing itself in any meaningful sense.

Participatory budgeting is a process in which community members directly decide how a portion of shared funds are allocated. It originated in Porto Alegre, Brazil in 1989, where the city government opened its capital budget to direct citizen decision-making. Residents proposed projects, deliberated, and voted. The results shifted spending toward neighborhoods that had historically been ignored. The process spread — to cities across Latin America, Europe, and eventually the United States, and to institutions well beyond municipal government.

The evidence on participatory budgeting is consistent: it produces more equitable outcomes than expert-administered budgeting, it increases civic participation (especially among people who don't typically engage with formal political processes), and it builds trust in institutions. People who participate in deciding how resources are spent are more invested in the outcomes.

In this framework, the community's shared funds — generated by demurrage and other commons contributions — are allocated through participatory budgeting. Members propose expenditures. The community deliberates. Members vote. The decisions are binding.

This is not advisory input into a decision made by someone else. It is the decision.

## How It Works in Practice

The governance model in this framework has several layers, and they serve different functions.

**The general assembly** is the whole community. Major decisions — changes to the constitution, significant resource allocations, membership in the federation — go to the assembly. Every member has a vote. This is the foundation of legitimacy.

**The council** handles ongoing governance between assemblies. Council members are drawn by sortition from the community's guilds — the organized bodies of members working in a shared domain. Sortition means selection by lot: the council is not elected, it is drawn, like jury duty. This eliminates campaigning, reduces the advantage of money and charisma, and ensures the council reflects the community rather than its most politically ambitious members.

**Officers** handle specific administrative functions — managing the bank, coordinating a domain, keeping records. These are appointed or elected for defined terms. They execute decisions; they do not make them unilaterally.

**Proposals** are how decisions get made. Any member can put a proposal to the community. The community deliberates and votes. The threshold for passage depends on the significance of the decision — ordinary operational matters require a simple majority, constitutional changes require more.

The budget is a proposal like any other. Members bring spending proposals to the community. The community weighs them against each other and against available resources. The allocation that results reflects the community's actual priorities, not a bureaucrat's model of what the community should want.

---

## Why This Matters

The erosion of social capital described in the previous document is partly a consequence of exclusion from meaningful participation. People disengage from institutions that don't respond to them. They stop attending meetings where decisions have already been made. They stop voting in elections where the choices are pre-selected. Disengagement is rational when participation produces nothing.

Self-governance reverses this. When a community member's voice actually changes outcomes — when a proposal they made results in a garden, a clinic, a tool library — participation becomes worthwhile. The feedback loop runs in the other direction. Engagement produces results; results produce more engagement; the community's capacity to act grows.

This is not guaranteed. Communities can become captured by internal factions. Deliberation can become exhausting. Majorities can override minorities in ways that damage trust. These are real risks and the framework tries to mitigate them — through the constitutional protections, the council's sortition-based composition, and the explicit recognition that governance requires ongoing maintenance.

But the alternative — handing decisions over to representatives or administrators and hoping they act in the community's interest — has a known failure mode. Self-governance at least puts the community in a position to see what is happening and do something about it. That is the precondition for everything else.
