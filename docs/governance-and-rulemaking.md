# Community Governance and Rule-Making

## What a Law Actually Is

A law is a rule backed by the credible threat of force. The state enforces laws ultimately through physical coercion — arrest, imprisonment, seizure. What makes something a *law* rather than a *rule* is that refusal to comply results in escalating force being applied to your body or property.

That definition does not fit a community that governs by genuine consent. The commons does not have a police force or a prison. Its ultimate sanction is exclusion from the community — a meaningful consequence, but categorically different from state coercion. Intermediate sanctions are relational and restorative: loss of trust score, loss of position, mediation requirements, restorative obligations.

Calling community rules "laws" imports expectations that don't belong here — expectations of enforcement by force, of legal specialists as interpreters, of punishment as the primary response to violation. The language shapes the culture. The commons uses more precise vocabulary.

## The Vocabulary

### Constitution

The foundational document of the community. It defines:
- What the community is and who may be a member
- What rights every member holds that no majority can vote away
- What the commons is obligated to provide
- How governance is structured
- How the constitution itself may be changed

The constitution is the hardest document to change. Amendments require a supermajority of the general assembly, a mandatory public comment period, and a waiting period between proposal and vote. It is not a living document in the sense of being casually revised — it is the bedrock.

### Covenant

A mutual promise between members and the community. Covenants establish obligations that run in both directions: the commons commits to provide housing, food, healthcare, and education; the member commits to participate honestly and contribute to the community's function.

Covenants are relational in character. Violating a covenant is not primarily a criminal act — it is a breach of a relationship that requires repair. The first response is mediation and restoration, not punishment.

### Statute

A formally enacted operating rule, adopted through a defined deliberative process. Statutes govern how things work within a domain or across the community: market hours, building standards, food handling procedures, membership processes.

Statutes are made by the body with the appropriate mandate:
- **Community statutes** — adopted by the community council, governing cross-domain matters
- **Domain statutes** — adopted by domain councils, governing their own domain's operations

Statutes are not permanent. All statutes carry a sunset date and expire unless affirmatively renewed. This forces regular review rather than accumulation of dead-letter rules.

### Standard

A technical specification defining what something must achieve or how it must be built. Standards govern water quality, building materials, food safety, electrical installations, and similar technical matters.

Standards are set by domain councils in consultation with technical staff. They are published, plain-language, and accessible to any member. Where external standards exist (public health thresholds, structural engineering requirements), the community adopts them by reference rather than reinventing them.

### Right

Something a member can affirmatively claim against the community. Rights are defined in the constitution and cannot be removed by statute or domain council action. Examples:
- The right to adequate food, housing, healthcare, and education
- The right to participate in governance
- The right to due process before any sanction is applied
- The right to appeal any domain statute to the community council

## The Three Governance Bodies

Every meaningful action in the community — every statute enacted, every budget allocated, every domain policy changed — requires authorization from one of three bodies. The bodies differ in size, selection, speed, and the kinds of decisions they are fit to make.

| Body | Size | Selection | Speed | Legitimacy |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Council** | 3–5 members | Sortition from domain-qualified pool | Fast | Domain expertise + random selection |
| **Assembly** | ~10–20% of community | Sortition from all active members | Medium | Broad representation |
| **Referendum** | All members | Direct vote | Slow | Unanimity of community voice |

These bodies are not competing power centers. They operate on different scales and specialize in different kinds of decisions. The default path is council → assembly → referendum as stakes increase.

---

### Councils

A council is the operating governance body for one or more functional domains. It makes day-to-day operational decisions: domain statutes, technical standards, resource allocation within its domain's budget, and responses to domain-level problems.

Councils have **3–5 seats**, filled by random draw from a **qualified sortition pool** — a roster of members with demonstrated knowledge and active participation in the relevant domain(s). The qualified pool removes the need for competitive elections while ensuring the people making decisions are the people doing the work.

**Multi-domain purview.** A single council may hold responsibility for more than one domain. In a small community it makes no sense to constitute a separate five-person council for each of twenty domains — the membership pool is too small and the overhead too high. Instead, related domains are grouped under a single council with a mandate that spans all of them.

Example groupings at community scale:

| Council | Domains |
|---|---|
| Sustenance | Food, Agriculture, Water |
| Care | Healthcare, Dependency Care, Child Care, Deathcare |
| Infrastructure | Housing, Energy, Sanitation, Transport |
| Commons | Education, Enrichment, Communications |
| Safety | Fire, Justice |
| Economy | Marketplace, Manufacturing, Courier |

As the community grows, a council's domains may be split and a new council constituted. The Sustenance Council might eventually become separate Food and Water & Sanitation councils. The split requires an assembly vote.

**Council decisions are binding within their domains.** A council does not need assembly or referendum approval for decisions within its mandate. It does need to escalate when:
- A decision materially affects members outside its domain(s)
- A decision conflicts with an existing statute from another council
- A decision exceeds the domain's budget envelope
- The constitution mandates a higher body for that action class
- Any member appeals to the assembly within the public comment window

---

### Assembly

The assembly is the community's cross-domain deliberative body. It is constituted by random draw from the full active membership — not qualified by domain, because its job is broad representation, not domain expertise.

**Size scales with community.** The assembly should represent roughly 10–20% of active members, with a floor of 9 and a ceiling appropriate to the community's deliberative capacity. A community of 50 has a 9-person assembly. A community of 200 has a 20–30 person assembly.

The assembly holds authority over:
- Inter-domain coordination and conflict
- Budget envelopes for each council
- Domain mandates — the charter each council operates under
- Membership decisions (admitting, suspending, excluding members)
- Decisions that require community-wide buy-in but fall short of the constitutional threshold
- Referrals from councils that have chosen to escalate

Assembly members serve a term (default: 6 months), then rotate back into the general membership pool. No member serves consecutive terms without a gap. This prevents the gradual formation of a permanent assembly class.

---

### Referendum

A referendum is a direct vote of all active members. It is the highest expression of community will, and is reserved for decisions that are either constitutionally mandated or too consequential to delegate.

Referenda are triggered by:
1. **Constitutional mandate** — certain action classes always require a referendum: changing the levy rate, amending the constitution, joining or leaving a federation, changing the demurrage rate
2. **Assembly escalation** — the assembly may choose to put a question to a community-wide vote rather than decide it themselves
3. **Member petition** — when a threshold of members (default: 20%) demand a referendum on any question, it must be scheduled

Referenda have a **deliberation window** (default: 7 days) before voting opens, a **voting window** (default: 7 days), and an optional **quorum requirement**. Results are recorded as binding community decisions.

Because referenda are slow and require high participation, their power comes from their rarity. A community that holds referenda every week has diluted the instrument. The constitution should be conservative about what triggers a mandatory referendum.

## The Escalation Path

The default flow for any governance decision is:

```
Council (operational, within domain)
    ↓  if cross-domain, budget-exceeding, or appealed
Assembly (cross-domain, policy, membership)
    ↓  if constitutionally mandated, assembly-escalated, or petitioned
Referendum (all members, binding)
```

Any member may appeal a council decision to the assembly during the public comment window. The assembly does not retry the decision from scratch — it reviews whether the council acted within its mandate. If it did, the appeal fails. If it didn't, the assembly remands or decides directly.

Any member may petition for a referendum on any assembly decision. If the petition threshold is met, the question goes to a community vote. The assembly decision is suspended pending the outcome.

## Specialization by Action Class

The constitution defines an **authority map** — a table specifying which body must authorize each class of action. This prevents ad-hoc power grabs and makes the governance structure legible to all members.

Examples:

| Action | Required authority |
|---|---|
| Change market day schedule | Council (Economy) |
| Change food handling standards | Council (Sustenance) |
| Allocate budget between domains | Assembly |
| Admit a new member | Assembly |
| Suspend a member pending review | Assembly |
| Change the commons levy rate | Referendum |
| Amend the constitution | Referendum (supermajority) |
| Join a federation | Referendum |
| Exclude a member permanently | Referendum |
| Declare a domain emergency | Council (immediate), Assembly (ratify within 72h) |

The authority map lives in the constitution and can only be changed by the body that would gain or lose authority — preventing any single body from quietly expanding its own mandate.

## Rule-Making Tiers

### Tier 1 — Constitutional

**What:** Foundational commitments, rights, governance structure, community identity.
**Made by:** Referendum — all members.
**Threshold:** Supermajority (3/4), mandatory 30-day comment period, ratification delay.
**Examples:** Member rights, commons obligations, governance structure.

### Tier 2 — Community Statutes

**What:** Cross-domain rules, domain mandates, membership processes.
**Made by:** Assembly.
**Threshold:** Simple majority for operating rules; 2/3 for domain mandates.
**Examples:** Domain budget envelopes, inter-domain coordination rules, membership criteria.

### Tier 3 — Domain Statutes and Standards

**What:** Operating rules within a council's mandate.
**Made by:** Council.
**Threshold:** Council vote (majority of seated members), with 7-day public comment period.
**Appealed to:** Assembly, on grounds of mandate violation or constitutional conflict only.
**Examples:** Market hours, food handling procedures, building standards, domain-internal resource allocation.

## Principles of Rule-Making

**Plain language.** Any rule that cannot be explained clearly to an ordinary member is sent back for redrafting. Legal complexity is a power tool that concentrates interpretation authority. The commons has no need for it.

**Single subject.** One rule, one topic. No bundling unrelated provisions into a single vote.

**Mandatory sunset.** All statutes expire unless renewed. Domain statutes: 3-year default. Community statutes: 5-year default. Constitutional provisions do not sunset but are reviewed in a periodic constitutional convention.

**Public comment.** Every proposed statute is published to all members before adoption. Constitutional amendments: 30 days minimum. Community statutes: 14 days. Domain statutes: 7 days.

**Active pruning.** A periodic review (every 3 years) identifies contradictions, expired rules still on the books, and obsolete statutes. The community council is responsible for maintaining a clean, coherent rule set.

**Separation of functions.** Making rules, enforcing rules, and adjudicating disputes about rules are three separate functions held by three separate bodies. No single body does all three.

## Enforcement and Adjudication

Enforcement of domain statutes is the responsible council's job — it administers and applies its own rules. Disputes about whether a rule was violated, or whether a rule is constitutional, go to a citizen panel selected by sortition.

Citizen panels are randomly selected from eligible members, serve for a single case, and cannot be appealed except on grounds of procedural error. There are no professional judges. The panel reads the rule in plain language and decides whether it was violated and what restoration is owed.

Sanctions progress from least to most severe:
1. Acknowledgment and restoration — the member acknowledges the breach and makes it right
2. Mediated agreement — a structured process with a neutral facilitator
3. Temporary loss of specific privileges (position, marketplace access, etc.)
4. Trust score reduction — recorded in the member's history
5. Suspension from community participation pending assembly review
6. Exclusion — the last resort, requiring a full community referendum and member right of appeal

Physical coercion is not a tool the commons uses. If a situation requires physical intervention — assault, genuine emergency — that is a matter for the justice domain's conflict response function, not the rule-making system.

---

## What This System Is Not

It is not a legal system in the state sense. It produces no criminal records, no incarceration, no fines payable to the community treasury. It does not interface with state law enforcement except where members choose to involve external authorities in matters that rise to that level.

It is a governance system for a community that has chosen to live together under shared commitments. The foundation is consent, not coercion. The goal of every process is repair and continuation of relationship, not punishment and exclusion — except where the community's safety or integrity leaves no other option.
