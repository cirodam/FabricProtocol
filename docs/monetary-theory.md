# Monetary Theory

## The Core Constraint

Money is a claim on real things. You can create as many claims as you want, but if the real things aren't there, the claims are worthless. The constraint is never the money supply — it's the productive capacity behind it.

In this system that constraint is demographic. A population of N people needs approximately X calories, Y medical interventions, and Z square footage of shelter per year. These are not guesses — they are demographic facts. The need is a function of the population, and the population is what backs the currency. The money supply and the productive capacity it needs to represent are anchored to the same thing.

This means the federation's total productive capacity requirement is just a function of total population. If you know how many people you contain, you know roughly what you need to produce. The planning problem is demographic, not economic.

## Two Currencies, Two Layers

**Kin** is issued by the local community's central bank, anchored to its own population. It is the medium of exchange for daily life within the community.

**Kithe** is issued by the federal central bank, anchored to the total population of all member communities. It is the medium of inter-community trade and federation-level settlement. Most members never handle kithe directly — it is banking infrastructure, not pocket money.

## The Floor

Every community has a sovereign kin supply anchored to its person-years — one unit of kin issued per person per year of membership. This floor is inviolable. It cannot be eaten into by federation mechanics, trade imbalances, or monetary correction. A community can lose all its kithe reserves and still have a functioning internal economy.

When a community joins the federation, the federal central bank issues kithe against the new population as an endowment. The community can then issue kin on top of its person-years floor using that kithe as additional backing. The floor is always there regardless. The kithe-backed layer is optional expansion earned through federation participation.

This creates a genuine incentive to federate: joining expands your monetary capacity without threatening your baseline.

## Demand-Based Issuance

The food bank model is the primary mechanism for pulling essential goods toward communities that need them.

A community's food bank — or equivalent domain officer — can mint kithe against a documented need. A supplier who delivers the needed goods receives the freshly minted kithe as payment. The currency is spendable across the federation, giving the supplier real purchasing power in exchange for real goods delivered.

The lifecycle for consumables is self-liquidating:

1. Need documented → kithe minted → posted as an offer to suppliers
2. Supplier delivers goods → receives kithe
3. Members purchase from the food bank using kin → kin retired on redemption
4. Money supply returns to baseline

The kin exists only as long as the goods are in the storehouse. When the food is gone, the kin that paid for it is gone too. No permanent inflation. The mechanism works because consumption is the retirement event.

This only works cleanly for consumables. Durables — computers, infrastructure, equipment — have no natural retirement event and require a different model (see Investment Issuance below).

## The Officer Role

The entire system's integrity depends on physical verification at the boundary between the real world and the ledger. The food bank officer, or equivalent domain officer, is not just managing inventory — they are operating a monetary valve.

- Issuance is authorized only on confirmed physical receipt of goods
- Retirement is triggered only on confirmed consumption

Automation can track balances and execute transactions. It cannot verify physical reality. The officer is the bridge. This is a position of significant trust — equivalent to a warehouse receipt issuer in commodity finance, historically one of the most carefully governed roles in trade.

## The Productive Layer

Beyond the floor, kithe supply can expand through successful inter-community trade. When Community A fulfills Community B's food need:

- A genuine need was met
- Surplus that might have gone to waste found a use
- The federation functioned as intended

A small issuance bonus to the supplying community rewards the behavior the federation wants to incentivize: developing productive capacity and sharing surplus rather than hoarding. Communities that consistently produce and deliver needed goods accumulate a modest monetary reward for that role.

The reverse also applies: when a community has surplus it no longer needs, surrendering it burns kithe. Need and surplus are symmetric signals. The federation's money supply becomes a real-time measure of how well production matches need — not a measure of capital deployment or GDP.

## Progressive Correction

When the federation's money supply needs to contract — because more was minted than productive capacity justifies — the correction is applied starting from the communities with the largest kithe reserves above their population floor.

This is automatic and mechanical, not a political decision. The federation does not vote on who absorbs the correction. Whoever has the most above their floor yields first. Correction stops at the floor. No community can be reduced below its person-years backing.

The implications:

- Wealthy communities are the federation's shock absorbers by default
- Holding excess kithe above the floor is implicitly accepting that role
- Crisis response does not require consensus — the monetary mechanism responds before anyone finishes debating
- The correction is not confiscation — it is a supply contraction applied where there is room to absorb it

## The Insurance Framing

The demand-based issuance and progressive correction mechanisms together constitute a mutual food security guarantee — structurally identical to insurance.

- **Claims** — a community in crisis posts a food need, the pool pays out kithe to fund it
- **Cap** — the federation sets a maximum issuance ceiling above the reserve, preventing unlimited claims
- **Underwriting** — communities with documented need and verified delivery history can draw more than ones with no established record

No private insurer extracts profit from the pool. No government administers it. The communities are the pool. The monetary mechanism handles funding and claims automatically. The governance layer sets the rules and audits the officers.

## Insurance Pool Funding

The insurance pool is funded automatically at the moment of growth — no separate premium collection required.

For every person-year that enters the federation, two allocations are made simultaneously:

- **1.0 kin** → the community's local endowment (the floor)
- **0.1 kithe** → the federation insurance pool

The community always receives more than the pool. The floor is primary. The pool allocation is a fixed ratio of the same issuance event that creates the liability — new members mean new potential claims, and new members also fund the reserve to cover them.

At a federation of 1,000,000 person-years, the pool holds 100,000 kithe — 10% of total federation person-years value. This is squarely within the range of sound actuarial reserve practice. The reserve depth scales automatically with federation size.

When members leave or communities depart, both the floor and the pool contract symmetrically. The pool never holds reserves against a population that no longer exists.

The 0.1 ratio is a governance parameter. The federation can raise it if observed claim rates deplete the pool, or lower it and return the difference to communities if the reserve consistently exceeds what is needed.

## Investment Issuance

For capital goods — infrastructure, equipment, durable assets — the self-liquidating model does not apply. There is no consumption event to trigger retirement.

Community investment in durables is authorized through governance. The community collectively decides the acquisition is worth a permanent expansion of the money supply — justified by the productive value of the asset. If the investment genuinely increases productive capacity, the expansion is non-inflationary. If it does not, demurrage slowly corrects the oversupply.

This requires tighter governance than consumable issuance, because the consequences are permanent.

## Demurrage as Backstop

Demurrage is not a routine feature of the currency. It is a corrective tool activated when the supply runs above what productive capacity justifies — particularly after investment issuance that did not produce the expected value, or after orphaned currency from failed deliveries.

At the federation level, progressive correction handles most rebalancing automatically. Demurrage is the backstop for cases the automatic mechanism cannot reach.

## Why This Is Simpler Than Conventional Finance

Conventional financial infrastructure — reserves, reinsurance, bonds, derivatives, solvency requirements — exists almost entirely because no one can create or destroy money. All of it is elaborate plumbing to move a scarce resource between parties with different risk profiles, without anyone losing it permanently.

When issuance and retirement are first-class operations, most of that complexity dissolves. You do not need reserves held against future claims — you mint when the claim arrives. You do not need reinsurance — the federation mints against the need directly. You do not need solvency requirements — you cannot run out of money you can create.

The remaining complexity is governance: who authorizes issuance, what counts as a valid claim, how premiums are calibrated. That complexity is democratic and transparent. A community can understand and vote on those rules.

The simplicity is not incidental. It is what happens when a monetary system is designed around human needs rather than around the conservation of a scarce commodity.
