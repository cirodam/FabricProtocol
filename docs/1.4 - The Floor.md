# 1.4 The Floor

The argument up to this point has been largely about what is broken and why. This document is about something simpler: what a community owes its members, and why it is capable of providing it.

The answer to the first question is short. A community should make sure that everyone sleeping within it has a place to sleep, food to eat, and access to care when they are sick or injured. That's the floor — the minimum below which no member falls.

The answer to the second question is also short, though it is more often contested. An ordinary community of people already has what it needs to provide these things. It has people who can grow food, build shelter, and care for the sick. It has land. It has labor. What it typically lacks is not capacity but coordination — and that is a problem this framework is designed to solve.

---

## This Is Not a New Idea

Humans have sustained each other through communal organization for most of their existence. The idea that individuals or nuclear families are the natural unit of economic life is historically recent and geographically narrow. Across most of human history, meeting basic needs has been a collective project.

What the floor commits to is not utopian. It is the restoration of something ordinary: the expectation that a community takes care of its people.

---

## Shelter

Everyone needs a place to sleep. This is not complicated.

A community that has land and labor can build housing. A community that already has housing can ensure it is occupied rather than vacant. The obstacles to shelter in the formal economy are almost never physical — there are almost always enough materials and enough people who know how to build. The obstacles are financial and legal: debt, ownership concentration, speculation, zoning. These are institutional arrangements, not facts of nature, and a community organizing its own economy can work around them.

The floor does not specify what housing looks like. It specifies that no member of the community sleeps without one.

---

## Food

A community with land can grow food. The amount of land required to feed a person is small. The knowledge required is old and widely distributed. The labor required, while real, is not prohibitive when distributed across a community rather than concentrated in industrial monocultures.

The barriers to food security in contemporary communities are the same as the barriers to housing: financial and logistical, not physical. Most communities could feed themselves at a basic level if they organized to do so. Most don't, not because it's impossible, but because the incentives and infrastructure of the formal economy point elsewhere.

The floor does not require a community to be fully self-sufficient in food. It requires that no one in the community goes hungry.

---

## Healthcare

Healthcare is more complex than shelter or food. Some care requires training and equipment that a small community cannot easily produce internally. A community cannot bootstrap a cardiac surgery unit from scratch.

But most healthcare is not cardiac surgery. Most of what keeps people alive and functional is primary care: treatment of infections, management of chronic conditions, prenatal care, mental health support, basic surgery, wound care, dental work. These can be provided by people with tractable amounts of training using affordable equipment. A community with a trained nurse practitioner and a modest formulary can meet most of the day-to-day healthcare needs of its members.

The floor does not promise the full range of modern medicine. It promises that illness and injury do not go untreated for lack of resources.

---

## Why the Floor Comes First

A community that has not guaranteed the floor has not yet secured the precondition for everything else.

People who are hungry cannot think clearly. People who are sleeping outdoors cannot participate fully in community life. People who are sick and untreated become sicker. The floor is not a luxury program — it is the baseline of community function. Everything built on top of it depends on it.

The floor also changes the structural incentives described in the previous document. A person who knows they will not starve, will not lose their housing, and will receive care when sick is a person with real alternatives. They can refuse bad conditions. They can take risks. They can contribute to the community without fear that failure means catastrophe. The floor is not charity. It is the foundation of a functional economy.

---

## Capacity Already Exists

The most important thing to understand about the floor is that providing it is within the ordinary capacity of a community of people.

This is not self-evident to people who have internalized the logic of the formal economy, where everything has a price and providing anything requires money that must first be acquired. But money is not what feeds people or builds houses or delivers babies. People do those things. And communities have people.

The constraint is not physical. It is organizational. Building an organization capable of meeting basic needs is exactly what this framework is for.
