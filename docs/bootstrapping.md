# Bootstrapping a Community

This document covers two scenarios: bootstrapping during a crisis (collapse of existing systems) and bootstrapping under normal conditions (building a community proactively, before crisis arrives). The normal-conditions path is covered first, because that is the more common situation and because communities built before a crisis are far better positioned to survive one.

---

# Part One: Peacetime Bootstrapping

## The Core Insight

The hardest part of building a new community isn't the ideology or the governance or the currency theory. It is the chicken-and-egg problem: a marketplace with no sellers is useless; a currency with nothing to buy is useless; a mutual aid network with no members is theater. Each piece only becomes valuable once others exist.

The solution is sequencing. Start with the one thing that creates value immediately for individuals, with no prior community infrastructure required. Then let each layer make the next one attractive.

The entry point is the marketplace.

---

## Stage 1: The Market — First Contact

### Why markets first

A marketplace asks almost nothing of participants philosophically. You do not need to believe in the community vision, trust strangers deeply, or understand the currency theory. You need something to trade and something you want.

The kin serves as a lubricant. It solves the coincidence-of-wants problem that makes barter impractical — if you have eggs and want firewood, and I have firewood and want childcare, barter fails. Kin bridges that. Both transactions can happen independently.

### The market day ritual

Rather than a passive always-on listing board, the community holds regular **market days** — a recurring scheduled event. The schedule creates the activity. Both buyers and sellers prepare because they know when to show up.

Two market days per week is the recommended default:

- **Saturday market** (9am–1pm) — the main weekly market. Leverages existing farmers market culture; people already have this habit. Broader selection: produce, preserved food, goods, crafts, services.
- **Wednesday exchange** (4pm–7pm) — lighter mid-week session. Labor hours, prepared food, small goods, quick transactions. Fills needs that arise during the week without waiting until Saturday.

The schedule is seeded automatically when the system starts for the first time.

### What the commonwealth does at this stage

Before a real market exists, the **commonwealth can post standing buy orders** for things members already produce. This gives early producers a guaranteed buyer and creates immediate liquidity for the currency.

Examples:
- "Commonwealth will buy eggs at X kin/dozen, up to Y dozen per week"
- "Commonwealth will buy firewood at X kin/cord"
- "Commonwealth will pay X kin/hour for childcare"

These buy orders do not require a budget approved in advance — the central bank can fund them directly as an endowment to the commonwealth. This is the first real thing kin can buy, which makes the currency real.

### What success at Stage 1 looks like

- People attend market days regularly
- Transactions are happening in kin
- Members have kin balances and are spending them
- New people are joining to participate in the market
- The currency feels real because it buys real things

This is the foundation. Everything else is built on top of it.

---

## Stage 2: Labor Exchange — Deeper Interdependence

Once people have a trading relationship and feel comfortable with kin, the natural next step is labor exchange. This is the move from "I sell you a thing" to "I help you do a thing."

Labor exchange is qualitatively different from goods trade:
- It requires spending time together
- It builds personal relationships and trust
- It reveals who is reliable, skilled, and generous
- It creates a sense of mutual obligation that persists

### How it enters naturally

Someone posts on the marketplace: "Offering 4 hours of carpentry, 200 kin." Someone buys it. The work gets done. Both parties now have a relationship that did not exist before.

The community does not need to organize this — it emerges from the marketplace once labor posts are normalized. The role of the system is to make posting and fulfilling labor offers easy.

### What success at Stage 2 looks like

- Labor posts appear regularly on the marketplace
- Members are paying each other in kin for work
- Social relationships are forming around economic activity
- People are noticing that the community has skills they didn't know about

---

## Stage 3: Commonwealth Integration — Collective Capacity

At some point, individual transactions are not enough. Some needs require collective resources: a community kitchen, organized farming coordination, delivery routes to people who cannot leave their homes. This is where the **commonwealth** becomes active beyond buy orders.

### The natural first expansion: food

Food is almost always the first domain to coalesce, because it is the most universal need and because the market already has food producers in it. The progression is natural:

**Market → Community Kitchen**

Several people are already selling prepared food at market days. They are each cooking separately, cleaning separately, buying ingredients separately. A community kitchen lets them share equipment, coordinate prep, pool ingredient purchasing, and produce more than they could individually. The commonwealth funds the kitchen space and equipment; the cooks pay a small kin fee per use or work a minimum number of community meals in exchange.

The community kitchen also produces the most visible commons good: free or heavily subsidized meals for members who are struggling. This is the first thing most members will point to when asked what the community does.

**Farmers → Farmers Council**

The food producers at the market — gardeners, smallholders, anyone growing food — benefit from coordination: shared seed purchasing, coordinated crop planning so the market has variety rather than everyone growing tomatoes, collective storage, and mutual aid during planting and harvest. A farmers council formalizes this coordination. It is not a governing body — it is a peer group that makes collective decisions about production planning and shares resources.

The council reports to the food domain. Its standing buy orders through the commonwealth give farmers a guaranteed floor price for staple crops, which reduces the risk of producing for the community rather than just for personal use.

**Kitchen + Farmers Council → Courier Food Routes**

Rural counties have a well-understood problem: elderly people, people with disabilities, people recovering from illness, single parents overwhelmed with young children — people who cannot easily get to the market and cannot easily feed themselves. In many counties, churches and nonprofits already run informal meal delivery; they are always understaffed and underfunded.

The community's courier domain can take this on formally. Courier members deliver meals from the community kitchen and food boxes from the farmers council on regular routes. Delivery workers earn kin per route. Recipients receive food at zero or minimal kin cost — the commons covers the gap.

This is a critical function for two reasons. First, it is genuinely necessary — these people exist in every rural community and are currently underserved. Second, it is visible. Everyone knows someone who benefits from a meal route. It makes the community's value concrete and human.

### Why this sequence answers the demurrage question

**Demurrage** — the small periodic fee on kin holdings above the baseline — is the mechanism that funds the commons. Kin held idle slowly returns to the community pool. When people ask what demurrage is for, the answer is not an abstraction about monetary theory. It is:

> "The community kitchen that runs three days a week. The meal routes that deliver to people who can't get out. The seed cooperative that lets farmers plan together. The storage facility that keeps the harvest from rotting."

Demurrage is how the community taxes the surplus to fund collective goods. The goods it funds should be visible, valued, and human-scale enough that members can name specific people who benefit. A meal route that delivers to known neighbors is the best possible answer to "why am I paying this fee."

This is also why the food system should be the *first* domain stood up: it produces the most legible benefits. People understand food. They understand that an elderly neighbor getting a hot meal is a good use of community resources. Once that legitimacy is established, the community's capacity to fund other domains through the same mechanism is accepted.

### What the system provides

- A domain budget funded by the commonwealth levy (collected automatically from all accounts)
- A council to make domain-level decisions
- A sortition pool for governance once the domain is mature
- Integration with payroll so domain workers earn kin automatically

### What success at Stage 3 looks like

- The community kitchen is operating, producing meals for the market and for meal routes
- A farmers council is coordinating crop planning and sharing resources
- Courier routes are delivering food to members who cannot get to the market
- Members can name specific people who benefit from the commons
- When asked what demurrage is for, any member can give a concrete answer

## Stage 4: Courier and Transport — Physical Infrastructure

The food delivery routes established in Stage 3 prove something important: the community has people willing to drive, a coordination system that works, and members who depend on it. That is the seed of a general-purpose logistics network.

### Expanding the courier domain

Once food routes are running, the courier domain naturally absorbs other delivery needs:

- **Goods delivery** — marketplace buyers who can't make it to market day can place orders and have them delivered. Sellers expand their effective reach beyond whoever shows up in person.
- **Inter-household exchange** — members trading labor or goods who live across the county can use couriers rather than each making a separate trip.
- **Supply runs** — the community kitchen, the farmers council, and other domains need materials. Consolidated supply runs in a single vehicle replace ten individual trips.
- **Medical and prescription delivery** — once trust is established, couriers can pick up prescriptions and medical supplies for homebound members.

Each delivery earns the courier kin. The recipient pays in kin, or the commons covers it for members who cannot pay. The courier domain tracks routes, schedules, and capacity.

### Why this matters beyond convenience

In a rural county, physical distance is the primary barrier to community participation. A 20-mile round trip to the market is trivial for someone with a reliable vehicle and flexible time. It is prohibitive for someone elderly, disabled, carless, or working two jobs.

A courier network removes that barrier. It means the community's reach is not limited to who can physically show up. This expands membership, expands the market, and deepens the community's role in members' actual lives.

It also means the community has a logistics capability that becomes critical in a crisis. A network that delivers groceries and marketplace goods in normal times can deliver medicine, emergency supplies, and information when things go wrong.

### Transport: the next layer

Courier covers goods. Transport covers people.

Rural communities have a well-documented transportation problem: no public transit, aging population, medical appointments requiring long drives, young people without licenses, workers who can't afford car repairs. The informal solution — neighbors driving each other — already exists everywhere. The community formalizes and sustains it.

A **transport domain** coordinates:

- **Scheduled routes** — regular runs between population centers (town, neighboring county seat, medical centers), on predictable days and times
- **On-demand rides** — members who need a specific trip post a request; available drivers respond
- **Medical transport** — rides to appointments, dialysis, chemotherapy, specialist visits — currently one of the most underserved needs in rural counties
- **Market transport** — pickup routes that bring members without cars to and from market days, dramatically increasing attendance

Drivers earn kin per mile or per trip. Riders pay in kin; the commons subsidizes rides for members who cannot pay. Vehicles owned by members who join the transport domain are maintained partly by the community in exchange for availability.

### The dollar connection

Transport has a real dollar cost — fuel, maintenance, insurance. This is one of the clearest arguments for the provisioning domain running a dollar interface. The community earns dollars collectively (through provisioning), converts them into fuel and parts, and pays drivers in kin. Members who drive are contributing real dollar-denominated value; the community honors that through kin earnings and vehicle maintenance support.

### What success at Stage 4 looks like

- Courier routes cover the whole community geography, not just the market location
- Homebound members can access the marketplace without leaving home
- Medical transport is running for members who need it
- The transport domain is self-sustaining: kin earnings plus commons subsidy cover operating costs
- The community's physical reach matches its membership, regardless of who has a car

## Stage 5: Governance — People Who Care About the Rules

Governance is the last thing people adopt, not the first. This is correct and healthy. People engage with governance when they have something at stake — when they care about the community because the community has given them something they value.

By Stage 4, members:
- Have regular market relationships
- May earn part of their income in kin
- May depend on a domain for food, childcare, or another essential service
- Have social relationships built through the community

These people will care about decisions. They will attend assembly meetings. They will engage with constitutional questions. They will contest proposals they disagree with and support ones they believe in.

Governance introduced at Stage 1 is theater — nobody has enough stake to engage seriously. Governance at Stage 4 is real, because people have real stakes.

### The constitution in peacetime

The constitution does not need to be written under emergency conditions. A founding group can adopt a minimal temporary constitution at the start — enough to authorize market days and commonwealth accounts — and revise it through deliberate assembly processes as the community grows and develops shared values.

This is a significant advantage over crisis bootstrapping, where the constitution must be drafted under stress and time pressure.

---

## Stage 6: Federation — Connecting Communities

A community that is functioning well will eventually encounter limits that only inter-community coordination can solve:

- A skill that does not exist locally
- A surplus in one domain that another community needs
- A disaster that requires outside mutual aid
- A political threat that requires collective response

This is when federation becomes valuable. A federation is not a government — it is a network of mutual commitments between self-governing communities.

The clearing system allows inter-community trade in kin. Federated backup replicates critical data across nodes. The congress of federations provides a mechanism for decisions that require multi-community coordination.

## The Realistic Timeline

These stages are not months — they are years, in a peacetime context where no emergency is forcing rapid change.

| Stage | Rough timeline | Milestone |
|-------|---------------|-----------|
| 1. Market | Month 1–6 | Regular market days, transactions happening |
| 2. Labor exchange | Month 3–12 | Labor posts normalized, social relationships forming |
| 3. Food domain | Month 6–18 | Kitchen running, meal routes delivering, farmers coordinating |
| 4. Courier + transport | Month 12–24 | Goods delivery and medical transport operating |
| 5. Active governance | Year 2–3 | Assembly attendance meaningful, constitution revised |
| 6. Federation | Year 3–5 | First inter-community trade, mutual aid established |

The sequence can compress dramatically if crisis accelerates it. See Part Two for the crisis path.

## Contingent Domains — Activating When Official Systems Fail

Not all domains need to be built from scratch. Some exist in parallel with official systems that currently function — and should only be activated when those systems degrade below the level the community can tolerate.

These domains are built into the system as dormant capabilities. A community that activates them prematurely wastes resources and creates political friction with existing institutions. A community that waits too long to activate them leaves members without a fallback when those institutions fail.

The correct posture is: **monitor, prepare, activate at threshold.**

---

### Healthcare

Rural healthcare is already in active degradation. Hospital closures, physician shortages, and insurance barriers are not hypothetical — they are current conditions in most of the communities this system is designed to serve. For many rural counties, the healthcare domain is not contingent at all; it is immediately necessary.

At minimum, the community should:
- Identify every member with medical training (physicians, nurses, EMTs, paramedics, dentists, veterinarians — veterinarians have broadly applicable clinical skills)
- Maintain a list of available medical equipment and supplies across the community
- Run a basic community health worker program — trained laypeople who can do wellness checks, manage chronic disease monitoring, and triage before escalating to formal care

When the local clinic closes or becomes inaccessible, this preparation becomes a functioning community clinic. The transition should not require starting from zero.

---

### Education

Public schools are chronically underfunded in rural counties and increasingly subject to political interference that may render them unable to fulfill their educational function. The education domain activates progressively rather than all at once:

- **Supplementary** (official system functioning): after-school programs, tutoring, skill-sharing workshops, adult literacy, vocational training the schools don't offer
- **Parallel** (official system degrading): full alternative schooling for families who opt out, homeschool support networks, community-run curricula
- **Primary** (official system failed or closed): the community school becomes the main educational institution

The library is the anchor at every level — it functions as a community learning center regardless of what the school system is doing.

---

### Fire

Most rural communities already have volunteer fire departments, and the community should not duplicate them. The fire domain activates if and when the volunteer department collapses from lack of funding or membership, or if the community grows beyond the department's coverage area.

In the interim: recruit community members into the existing volunteer department. A community with deep membership in the local fire department is better positioned than one that has built a parallel structure. The domain exists as a fallback, not a replacement.

---

### Deathcare

The funeral industry is largely inaccessible by cost for low-income communities — a basic funeral can cost $8,000–$15,000, and many families go into debt or cannot afford burial at all. This is a current problem, not a contingency.

The deathcare domain supports:
- **Home funerals and natural burial** — legal in most states, requiring no funeral home involvement. Community members trained in body preparation and burial can provide this.
- **Community burial ground** — a commons-held space for members who choose it
- **Grief support and ceremony** — the social and ritual functions that commercial funeral homes nominally provide but rarely do well

This domain does not require official system failure. It fills a gap that the current system leaves open for low-income members now.

---

### Justice

The formal justice system — courts, police, incarceration — will likely remain partially functional long after other systems degrade. The community does not attempt to replace it. What the community builds is a **conflict resolution capacity** for disputes between members that are better handled internally:

- Neighbor disputes over shared resources, noise, boundaries
- Marketplace disagreements about quality or payment
- Interpersonal conflicts that affect community function
- Disciplinary questions about member conduct that violates community norms

A community mediation process, grounded in the constitution and administered by a panel drawn from the sortition pool, handles these cases. It is not a court and does not pretend to be one. It is a community process for maintaining relationships and resolving disputes without escalating to state intervention.

The justice domain becomes more critical as trust in formal institutions erodes — and in communities with historically fraught relationships with policing, it may be immediately valued.

---

### The General Principle

A community building these contingent domains is not declaring war on existing institutions. It is building resilience. The goal is not to undermine what works, but to ensure that when something stops working — gradually, or suddenly — the community has a practiced alternative rather than a panicked improvisation.

The right time to build a fallback is before you need it.

---

## What Not to Do in the Peacetime Path

**Do not start with ideology.** People join because the community gives them something — a market, a service, a relationship. They adopt the values later, through participation. Leading with ideology selects for ideologues and repels the majority.

**Do not require deep commitment early.** The first ask should be trivial: come to the market on Saturday. If that goes well, the relationship deepens naturally. Asking for large commitments from strangers before trust is established will fail.

**Do not wait for the perfect moment.** The market can start with ten people and two tables. Governance can start with a one-page charter. Domains can start with three people and a shared tool. Perfection is the enemy of starting.

**Do not neglect the dollar interface.** In a functioning economy, members still need dollars. The provisioning domain — the community's controlled interface to the dollar economy — should be established early enough that members can earn dollars collectively and convert between kin and dollar-denominated goods.

# Part Two: Crisis Bootstrapping

## The Situation

The federal government has defaulted. The dollar has failed or is failing. State government has lost the capacity to function. Supply chains have broken down. The community you're in — a rural town that has been losing population and economic vitality for decades — is now on its own.

This is not a hypothetical to be planned for later. If you are reading this in that situation, this document is a practical sequence for the first weeks, months, and year. Priority order matters. Do not skip ahead.

What this community has that it didn't know it had:
- Land, much of it underused or abandoned
- People with practical skills — farming, building, cooking, medicine, mechanics
- Buildings — houses, barns, churches, schools, community centers, warehouses
- Equipment — tractors, trucks, tools, canning equipment, generators
- Social memory — people who remember how things used to work before they were outsourced

What it doesn't have yet:
- Organization
- Shared accounting
- A way to make collective decisions
- A clear picture of its own resources

That's what this document is for.

---

## Phase 1: The First Week — Stabilize and Count

### Gather

Someone has to call the first meeting. It doesn't matter who — a pastor, a farmer, a teacher, a town council member, whoever still has legitimacy and a way to reach people. Call a meeting at the largest available space. Get as many people there as possible.

The agenda for that first meeting is narrow:
1. Acknowledge the situation plainly
2. Agree to meet again in 48 hours
3. Identify three immediate needs: food, medical, security from outside threats
4. Ask who has relevant knowledge or resources for each

Do not try to form a government at this meeting. Do not vote on anything structural. You don't have enough information and people are scared. Just establish that the community is organizing and will meet again.

### Take Inventory

Within the first week, send small teams to do a basic inventory of:

**Food**
- What's in the grocery store, co-op, restaurant supply, and warehouse stocks
- What's in people's homes (ask, don't demand — trust matters now)
- What's growing in fields right now and when it will be ready
- What livestock exists and where
- What seeds are available

**Medical**
- Who are the doctors, nurses, EMTs, veterinarians, dentists in the community
- What medications and medical supplies exist (clinic, pharmacy, veterinary supply)
- What chronic conditions exist in the population that require ongoing medication

**Shelter**
- Which buildings are occupied and by whom
- Which buildings are empty, structurally sound, and could house more people
- What the heating situation is for each building going into the season

**Skills**
- Who can operate farm equipment
- Who has construction skills
- Who can preserve food (canning, smoking, drying)
- Who has medical training at any level
- Who has mechanical skills (engines, electrical, plumbing)

This inventory is not bureaucratic busywork. It is the foundation for every decision that follows. A community that doesn't know what it has cannot allocate it.

### Immediate Security

Not from other community members — from organized outside threats. This is a real concern in a collapse scenario. Establish a watch rotation of willing adults. The goal is early warning, not combat. Most threats are deterred by visible community organization.

Do not arm a militia or create a command structure at this stage. That path leads to the wrong outcomes. The community's long-term security comes from being large, organized, productive, and known to be so — not from being armed and aggressive.

---

## Phase 2: The First Month — Feed Everyone and Establish Basic Governance

### Food First

The inventory tells you what you have. Now calculate how long it lasts at current consumption.

Immediately:
- Pool the grain, legumes, and shelf-stable food into a central store with a rotating volunteer guard
- Establish a daily communal meal — not because private food is forbidden, but because it establishes that no one goes hungry and builds community cohesion
- Identify what fields can be planted now and plant them, prioritizing caloric density: potatoes, corn, beans, squash, winter wheat if the season allows

Appoint a food coordinator — not by election, by asking who knows the most about food production and preservation. This person does not govern; they coordinate information and logistics. The community ratifies this appointment at the next meeting.

If you are heading into winter, the food calculus is urgent. See the food stockpile and food production planning documents for caloric modeling. A community of 500 going into a northern winter needs roughly 500,000 calories per day — about 180 million calories to carry through a six-month winter. Know your number.

### Water

If municipal water is still running, use it. Establish a task force to understand the water system and what it takes to keep it running without grid power. Identify the pump stations, their power requirements, and what backup power exists.

If municipal water is not running, identify every well in the area. Test them. Establish water distribution points. Boil or treat any surface water. This is non-negotiable — waterborne illness will kill people faster than almost anything else.

### Medical

Establish a community medical station in the best available space — a clinic, a school, a church hall. Every person with medical training works there, sharing knowledge and dividing responsibilities by competence level. A doctor and a nurse and an EMT working together can do far more than any of them alone.

Inventory medications. Prioritize: insulin and diabetic supplies, cardiac medications, blood pressure medications, antibiotics, pain management, mental health medications. People who will die without their medication are the immediate concern.

Establish a triage system. The medical team decides on medical matters. The community supports them with resources.

### Basic Governance

By the end of the first month, the community needs a minimal decision-making structure. Not a full constitution — just enough to coordinate.

Convene a general assembly of all adults. At this meeting:

1. Adopt a temporary charter — a single page that says: we are organizing as a community, we will make decisions by assembly until we have time to build something better, we will not exclude anyone who is here and contributing, and we will revisit this charter in 90 days.

2. Appoint domain coordinators for the immediate critical domains: food, water, medical, shelter, security. These are coordinators, not commanders. They gather information, propose actions to the assembly, and execute what the assembly approves.

3. Establish a weekly assembly. Every adult can attend. Major decisions require assembly approval. Coordinators handle day-to-day operations within their domain.

This is not the permanent governance structure. It is the minimum viable structure to get through the emergency phase.

## Phase 3: Months Two Through Six — Build the Permanent Structure

The emergency is stabilizing. People are fed, housed, and receiving basic medical care. Now the community can take the time to build something that will last.

### Issue a Community Currency

The barter economy that emerged in Phase 1 is inefficient. People are tracking favors and debts informally, which creates friction and resentment.

Issue a community credit. The simplest implementation: a ledger maintained by a trusted bookkeeper (or two, for redundancy) that records kin earned and spent by each member. Kin are earned by contributing labor to community domains. Kin are spent at the community store, for services from other members, and for any goods the community produces.

The initial issuance is simple: every adult member receives a baseline endowment — enough to cover basic needs for 30 days. New kin enter the system when the community decides to fund something (pay domain workers, fund a new project). Kin leave the system through demurrage — a small monthly fee on holdings above the baseline, which flows back to the community pool.

This is not inflation. It is a tool for coordinating labor and distribution. See the central bank and currency backing documents for the full model.

### Write a Constitution

With the immediate emergency behind you, convene a constitutional assembly. This is not a quick meeting — it should take weeks, with drafts circulated, commented on, and revised. The constitution should address:

- Who is a member of this community and how membership is established
- What every member is guaranteed: food, shelter, healthcare, education
- What every member owes: labor contribution, honest participation, respect for community decisions
- How governance works permanently
- What rights no majority can vote away
- How the constitution can be changed

Take this seriously. The document you write now will shape the community's culture for decades.

### Stand Up the Domains

With currency in circulation and governance established, formally organize the functional domains: food production, food processing, healthcare, construction and housing, education, water and sanitation, energy, and whatever others the community needs.

Each domain:
- Has a coordinator elected or selected by the domain's qualified workers
- Maintains its own budget, funded by allocation from the community pool
- Sets its own operating standards, ratified by the community
- Reports to the general assembly

Workers in each domain earn kin for their labor. The community decides how much each domain is funded. Domains spend their allocation on labor, equipment, and materials.

### Build the Qualified Sortition Pools

As each domain stabilizes, establish its qualified sortition pool. The pool is the foundation for domain governance going forward — decisions about standards, resource allocation, and policy are made by randomly selected panels of qualified domain members rather than by permanent administrators.

See the qualified sortition document for the full model.

## Phase 4: Year One and Beyond — Resilience and Federation

### Redundancy

A community that depends on a single well, a single doctor, a single mechanic is fragile. Year one is about building redundancy into everything critical:
- Train a second person for every critical skill
- Maintain backup equipment for essential functions
- Diversify food production so no single crop failure is catastrophic
- Build seed stock and preserve genetic diversity in crops

### Reach Out

Other communities are going through the same process. Make contact. A federation of communities is more resilient than any community alone — you can trade food for medicine, share equipment, provide mutual aid in disasters, and coordinate on things that require more than local capacity.

The federation is not a government. It is a network of mutual commitments between communities that remain self-governing. See the federation and congress of federations documents for how this scales.

### Document Everything

The knowledge that exists in your community right now lives in people's heads. Those people will age, get sick, and die. Document the farming practices, the medical protocols, the construction standards, the governance decisions, the history of what worked and what didn't.

This is not bureaucracy. It is intergenerational memory. The community you are building is intended to outlast everyone currently in it.

---

## What Not to Do

**Do not let a strongman take over.** In the chaos of the first weeks, someone forceful and organized will try to establish personal authority. The community's long-term survival depends on resisting this. Concentrate on building institutions, not following individuals.

**Do not exclude people.** The instinct to protect resources by excluding outsiders is understandable and almost always wrong. Every able-bodied person who joins the community adds more productive capacity than they consume, if organized well. Exclusion creates enemies. Inclusion creates community.

**Do not hoard.** Individual hoarding in a collective emergency is corrosive to the trust the community depends on. Establish clear, fair distribution early and maintain it. People will accept hardship if they believe it is shared fairly. They will not accept it if they believe others are cheating.

**Do not skip governance.** The temptation in an emergency is to just do things and deal with structure later. Structure later never comes, and the informal power relationships that form in the emergency become the permanent ones. Build governance in parallel with survival, even if it's minimal at first.

**Do not recreate what failed.** The systems that collapsed were not inevitable. This is an opportunity to build something different — more resilient, more equitable, more human-scale. Take it seriously.
