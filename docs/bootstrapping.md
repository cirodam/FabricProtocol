# Bootstrapping a Community After Collapse

## The Situation

The federal government has defaulted. The dollar has failed or is failing. State government has lost the capacity to function. Supply chains have broken down. The community you're in — a rural town that has been losing population and economic vitality for decades — is now on its own.

This is not a hypothetical to be planned for later. If you are reading this in that situation, this document is a practical sequence for the first weeks, months, and year. Priority order matters. Do not skip ahead.

What this community has that it didn't know it had:
- Land, much of it underused or abandoned
- People with practical skills — farming, building, cooking, medicine, mechanics
- Buildings — houses, barns, churches, schools, community centers, warehouses
- Equipment — tractors, trucks, tools, canning equipment, generators
- Social memory — people who remember how things used to work before they were outsourced

What it doesn't have yet:
- Organization
- Shared accounting
- A way to make collective decisions
- A clear picture of its own resources

That's what this document is for.

## Phase 1: The First Week — Stabilize and Count

### Gather

Someone has to call the first meeting. It doesn't matter who — a pastor, a farmer, a teacher, a town council member, whoever still has legitimacy and a way to reach people. Call a meeting at the largest available space. Get as many people there as possible.

The agenda for that first meeting is narrow:
1. Acknowledge the situation plainly
2. Agree to meet again in 48 hours
3. Identify three immediate needs: food, medical, security from outside threats
4. Ask who has relevant knowledge or resources for each

Do not try to form a government at this meeting. Do not vote on anything structural. You don't have enough information and people are scared. Just establish that the community is organizing and will meet again.

### Take Inventory

Within the first week, send small teams to do a basic inventory of:

**Food**
- What's in the grocery store, co-op, restaurant supply, and warehouse stocks
- What's in people's homes (ask, don't demand — trust matters now)
- What's growing in fields right now and when it will be ready
- What livestock exists and where
- What seeds are available

**Medical**
- Who are the doctors, nurses, EMTs, veterinarians, dentists in the community
- What medications and medical supplies exist (clinic, pharmacy, veterinary supply)
- What chronic conditions exist in the population that require ongoing medication

**Shelter**
- Which buildings are occupied and by whom
- Which buildings are empty, structurally sound, and could house more people
- What the heating situation is for each building going into the season

**Skills**
- Who can operate farm equipment
- Who has construction skills
- Who can preserve food (canning, smoking, drying)
- Who has medical training at any level
- Who has mechanical skills (engines, electrical, plumbing)

This inventory is not bureaucratic busywork. It is the foundation for every decision that follows. A community that doesn't know what it has cannot allocate it.

### Immediate Security

Not from other community members — from organized outside threats. This is a real concern in a collapse scenario. Establish a watch rotation of willing adults. The goal is early warning, not combat. Most threats are deterred by visible community organization.

Do not arm a militia or create a command structure at this stage. That path leads to the wrong outcomes. The community's long-term security comes from being large, organized, productive, and known to be so — not from being armed and aggressive.

## Phase 2: The First Month — Feed Everyone and Establish Basic Governance

### Food First

The inventory tells you what you have. Now calculate how long it lasts at current consumption.

Immediately:
- Pool the grain, legumes, and shelf-stable food into a central store with a rotating volunteer guard
- Establish a daily communal meal — not because private food is forbidden, but because it establishes that no one goes hungry and builds community cohesion
- Identify what fields can be planted now and plant them, prioritizing caloric density: potatoes, corn, beans, squash, winter wheat if the season allows

Appoint a food coordinator — not by election, by asking who knows the most about food production and preservation. This person does not govern; they coordinate information and logistics. The community ratifies this appointment at the next meeting.

If you are heading into winter, the food calculus is urgent. See the food stockpile and food production planning documents for caloric modeling. A community of 500 going into a northern winter needs roughly 500,000 calories per day — about 180 million calories to carry through a six-month winter. Know your number.

### Water

If municipal water is still running, use it. Establish a task force to understand the water system and what it takes to keep it running without grid power. Identify the pump stations, their power requirements, and what backup power exists.

If municipal water is not running, identify every well in the area. Test them. Establish water distribution points. Boil or treat any surface water. This is non-negotiable — waterborne illness will kill people faster than almost anything else.

### Medical

Establish a community medical station in the best available space — a clinic, a school, a church hall. Every person with medical training works there, sharing knowledge and dividing responsibilities by competence level. A doctor and a nurse and an EMT working together can do far more than any of them alone.

Inventory medications. Prioritize: insulin and diabetic supplies, cardiac medications, blood pressure medications, antibiotics, pain management, mental health medications. People who will die without their medication are the immediate concern.

Establish a triage system. The medical team decides on medical matters. The community supports them with resources.

### Basic Governance

By the end of the first month, the community needs a minimal decision-making structure. Not a full constitution — just enough to coordinate.

Convene a general assembly of all adults. At this meeting:

1. Adopt a temporary charter — a single page that says: we are organizing as a community, we will make decisions by assembly until we have time to build something better, we will not exclude anyone who is here and contributing, and we will revisit this charter in 90 days.

2. Appoint domain coordinators for the immediate critical domains: food, water, medical, shelter, security. These are coordinators, not commanders. They gather information, propose actions to the assembly, and execute what the assembly approves.

3. Establish a weekly assembly. Every adult can attend. Major decisions require assembly approval. Coordinators handle day-to-day operations within their domain.

This is not the permanent governance structure. It is the minimum viable structure to get through the emergency phase.

## Phase 3: Months Two Through Six — Build the Permanent Structure

The emergency is stabilizing. People are fed, housed, and receiving basic medical care. Now the community can take the time to build something that will last.

### Issue a Community Currency

The barter economy that emerged in Phase 1 is inefficient. People are tracking favors and debts informally, which creates friction and resentment.

Issue a community credit. The simplest implementation: a ledger maintained by a trusted bookkeeper (or two, for redundancy) that records kin earned and spent by each member. Kin are earned by contributing labor to community domains. Kin are spent at the community store, for services from other members, and for any goods the community produces.

The initial issuance is simple: every adult member receives a baseline endowment — enough to cover basic needs for 30 days. New kin enter the system when the community decides to fund something (pay domain workers, fund a new project). Kin leave the system through demurrage — a small monthly fee on holdings above the baseline, which flows back to the community pool.

This is not inflation. It is a tool for coordinating labor and distribution. See the central bank and currency backing documents for the full model.

### Write a Constitution

With the immediate emergency behind you, convene a constitutional assembly. This is not a quick meeting — it should take weeks, with drafts circulated, commented on, and revised. The constitution should address:

- Who is a member of this community and how membership is established
- What every member is guaranteed: food, shelter, healthcare, education
- What every member owes: labor contribution, honest participation, respect for community decisions
- How governance works permanently
- What rights no majority can vote away
- How the constitution can be changed

Take this seriously. The document you write now will shape the community's culture for decades.

### Stand Up the Domains

With currency in circulation and governance established, formally organize the functional domains: food production, food processing, healthcare, construction and housing, education, water and sanitation, energy, and whatever others the community needs.

Each domain:
- Has a coordinator elected or selected by the domain's qualified workers
- Maintains its own budget, funded by allocation from the community pool
- Sets its own operating standards, ratified by the community
- Reports to the general assembly

Workers in each domain earn kin for their labor. The community decides how much each domain is funded. Domains spend their allocation on labor, equipment, and materials.

### Build the Qualified Sortition Pools

As each domain stabilizes, establish its qualified sortition pool. The pool is the foundation for domain governance going forward — decisions about standards, resource allocation, and policy are made by randomly selected panels of qualified domain members rather than by permanent administrators.

See the qualified sortition document for the full model.

## Phase 4: Year One and Beyond — Resilience and Federation

### Redundancy

A community that depends on a single well, a single doctor, a single mechanic is fragile. Year one is about building redundancy into everything critical:
- Train a second person for every critical skill
- Maintain backup equipment for essential functions
- Diversify food production so no single crop failure is catastrophic
- Build seed stock and preserve genetic diversity in crops

### Reach Out

Other communities are going through the same process. Make contact. A federation of communities is more resilient than any community alone — you can trade food for medicine, share equipment, provide mutual aid in disasters, and coordinate on things that require more than local capacity.

The federation is not a government. It is a network of mutual commitments between communities that remain self-governing. See the federation and congress of federations documents for how this scales.

### Document Everything

The knowledge that exists in your community right now lives in people's heads. Those people will age, get sick, and die. Document the farming practices, the medical protocols, the construction standards, the governance decisions, the history of what worked and what didn't.

This is not bureaucracy. It is intergenerational memory. The community you are building is intended to outlast everyone currently in it.

## What Not to Do

**Do not let a strongman take over.** In the chaos of the first weeks, someone forceful and organized will try to establish personal authority. The community's long-term survival depends on resisting this. Concentrate on building institutions, not following individuals.

**Do not exclude people.** The instinct to protect resources by excluding outsiders is understandable and almost always wrong. Every able-bodied person who joins the community adds more productive capacity than they consume, if organized well. Exclusion creates enemies. Inclusion creates community.

**Do not hoard.** Individual hoarding in a collective emergency is corrosive to the trust the community depends on. Establish clear, fair distribution early and maintain it. People will accept hardship if they believe it is shared fairly. They will not accept it if they believe others are cheating.

**Do not skip governance.** The temptation in an emergency is to just do things and deal with structure later. Structure later never comes, and the informal power relationships that form in the emergency become the permanent ones. Build governance in parallel with survival, even if it's minimal at first.

**Do not recreate what failed.** The systems that collapsed were not inevitable. This is an opportunity to build something different — more resilient, more equitable, more human-scale. Take it seriously.
