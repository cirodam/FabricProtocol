# The Congress of Federations

## Why It Exists

A network of federations that grows large enough to matter will attract attempts to coordinate, regulate, or dominate it from outside. States will seek to tax or regulate it. Corporations will seek to extract from it. If the network has no coordinating institution of its own, something worse will fill the vacuum.

The Congress of Federations exists to occupy that space before it can be occupied by something hostile — to provide the minimum coordination infrastructure the network needs to function at scale, without becoming the thing it is designed to prevent.

The model is the Hanseatic League: a confederation of merchant cities that coordinated trade, maintained shared infrastructure, and provided mutual defense for roughly four centuries without ever becoming a state. It held no territory, commanded no army, and had no power to tax. Its only enforcement lever was trade access — cities that violated the rules were excluded from the network. That was enough.

---

## What the Congress Does

The Congress of Federations is authorized to do exactly the following, and nothing else:

**1. Maintain the inter-federation clearing system**
Operate and audit the ledger that records inter-federation trade obligations. The clearing system uses the same FEC standard as intra-federation trade: **1 FEC = 1 kWh of verified energy production**. This single anchor applies at all levels above the community — no separate inter-federation currency, no conversion step, no exchange rate between layers. The Congress runs the same clearing engine as the federation layer, with federations as participants instead of communities.

**2. Resolve disputes between federations**
Provide a neutral arbitration process when two federations have a trade dispute or a disagreement about clearing balances. Rulings are binding on the specific dispute; they do not create precedent that expands Congress authority.

**3. Coordinate shared infrastructure**
Maintain inter-federation transport route agreements, shared communication network standards, and federated data backup protocols. These are technical coordination functions — the Congress sets the standard, communities implement it.

**4. Respond to external threats**
Coordinate a collective response when the network faces an external threat — legal, political, or physical — that affects multiple federations simultaneously. This is the most dangerous power and the one most likely to be abused. It requires unanimous consent of all member federations to activate.

**5. Maintain the founding document**
Amend the founding document of the Congress itself, which requires unanimous consent of all member federations. The default answer to "can the Congress do X?" is no. Authority is enumerated; everything not listed is reserved to the federations.

---

## What the Congress Cannot Do

These prohibitions are absolute and cannot be amended by any vote short of unanimous consent of all member federations:

- **Cannot override a federation's internal governance** — a federation's constitution is sovereign within its borders
- **Cannot override a community's internal governance** — the layered autonomy principle applies at every level
- **Cannot tax member federations or communities** — the Congress is funded by voluntary contributions proportional to federation population
- **Cannot compel a federation to accept or expel a community** — membership decisions at every level are voluntary
- **Cannot issue currency** — the FEC system is algorithmic and federation-operated; the Congress has no monetary authority
- **Cannot acquire territory** — the Congress is a coordination body, not a state
- **Cannot maintain a standing military force** — defense coordination is limited to mutual aid agreements between federations
- **Cannot accumulate authority through precedent** — rulings on specific disputes do not create binding law

## Structure

**Assembly**
Each member federation sends delegates to the Congress Assembly proportional to its total population. Small federations receive a minimum of one delegate regardless of size. Delegates carry mandates from their federation assemblies — they vote the position their federation reached, not their personal opinion. Delegates are immediately recallable by their federation assembly.

**Voting**
- Routine operational matters (clearing rules, infrastructure standards): simple majority of delegates
- Dispute resolution: two-thirds majority
- New authority or amendment to founding document: unanimous consent of all member federations

**Secretariat**
A small permanent staff handles record-keeping, clearing operations, and dispute administration. Staff are employees of the Congress, not delegates. The secretariat has no voting authority and no independent decision-making power.

**Audit**
An independent audit function, staffed by rotating representatives from member federations, reviews clearing records and secretariat operations annually. Reports are published to all member federations.

---

## Funding

The Congress is funded by voluntary contributions from member federations, assessed proportionally to total population. A federation that stops contributing loses voting rights in the Assembly but retains access to the clearing system (the clearing system is infrastructure, not a privilege). A federation that violates clearing rules loses clearing access regardless of contribution status.

The Congress maintains no reserve fund beyond what is needed for one year of operating costs. Surplus contributions are returned to member federations.

---

## Membership

Joining the Congress is voluntary. A federation that meets the membership criteria and agrees to the founding document may apply. Admission requires a two-thirds vote of the existing Assembly.

Leaving the Congress is unconditional. A federation may withdraw at any time with 90 days notice. Outstanding clearing obligations must be settled before clearing access is terminated.

Expulsion is the last resort, reserved for federations that persistently violate clearing rules or the founding document after arbitration has failed. Expulsion requires a two-thirds vote and terminates clearing access only — it does not sever diplomatic or social relationships between communities.

## The Failure Mode to Prevent

The European Union began as a trade coordination body. Over decades it accumulated regulatory authority until it could override national law. The mechanism was consistent: a crisis created pressure for coordination, coordination required a rule, the rule required enforcement, enforcement required authority. Each step was individually reasonable. The cumulative result was a supranational government that no citizen directly voted to create.

The Congress of Federations defends against this with three structural constraints:

1. **Enumerated powers** — the Congress can only do what this document explicitly authorizes. Ambiguity is resolved against the Congress, not in its favor.
2. **Unanimous consent for expansion** — any new authority requires agreement from every member federation. A majority cannot expand the Congress's reach over the objection of a minority.
3. **No enforcement mechanism beyond trade access** — the Congress cannot compel compliance through force, law, or economic pressure beyond restricting clearing access. This hard limit on coercive power prevents the accumulation of state-like authority.

The Congress should be the least powerful institution that can still do its job. If it is doing more than what is listed in this document, it has grown too large.
