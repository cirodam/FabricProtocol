# Healthcare

## Purpose

The healthcare domain provides medical care to community members as a right of membership, not a market transaction. There are no bills, no insurance authorizations, no collections. A member who is sick sees someone who can help them. The domain exists to make that true.

---

## Why the Current System Is the Way It Is

American healthcare is expensive, fragmented, and administratively overwhelming because it was never designed as a healthcare system. It evolved as a series of market responses to political constraints — employer-based insurance as a WWII wage control workaround, Medicare and Medicaid as political compromises, urgent care as a private equity response to primary care collapse, hospital consolidation as a response to billing leverage.

The result is a system where a significant fraction of every healthcare dollar goes to billing, authorization, collections, and compliance — none of which produces any health. Administrative overhead accounts for roughly 25–30% of US healthcare spending by most estimates. A community clinic has none of this. Every resource goes to care.

Urgent care exists because primary care collapsed. When insurance reimbursement rates for primary care visits were driven down relative to specialist procedures over decades, fewer physicians chose primary care and those who remained reduced availability. The average wait for a primary care appointment in the US is now over 20 days. Urgent care filled that gap for people who could pay. The community clinic with same-day access eliminates the reason urgent care exists.

---

## Scale

A commons community at full scale — 10,000 or more members — is the size of a small town. Historically, a town of that size supported a full hospital, several general practitioners, and meaningful specialist depth. That is the right reference point, not a rural clinic scraping by with minimal staffing.

At this scale the community can sponsor enough members through full medical training to staff a genuine medical facility. The artificial scarcity of physicians in the current system is a product of supply restrictions and training cost, not of how many people are actually capable of practicing medicine. A community that invests in training its own physicians — through the university and hospital node network — dissolves that scarcity over time.

## Staffing Model

| Role | Training | Scope |
|------|----------|-------|
| Community health worker | 1–2 years | Health education, chronic disease support, care coordination, first contact |
| Generalist physician | ~11 years | Primary care, acute illness, diagnosis, procedures, most of what members need |
| Specialist physician | 13–15 years | Trained and positioned within the community for common specialties |
| Referral specialists | — | Accessed via federation network for rare presentations the community doesn't carry |

**No nurse practitioners or physician assistants as substitutes for physicians.** That model exists in the current system because physicians are artificially scarce and expensive. A community that sponsors its own members through full medical training produces physicians — the real thing, not a cost-cutting workaround. Community health workers handle coordination, education, and first contact. Physicians handle clinical care.

At 10,000 members the physician-to-patient ratio becomes reasonable. The standard panel size in well-resourced primary care is 1,500–2,000 patients. A community of 10,000 needs five to seven generalist physicians plus specialists in the conditions most prevalent in the population — which are knowable in a community that knows its members.

## The Clinic

**Panel-based care.** Each member has a physician who knows them, their history, and their context. Not a stranger every visit. Continuity of care is itself therapeutic and dramatically reduces unnecessary testing and misdiagnosis.

**Same-day access.** A portion of each day's schedule is reserved for acute needs. No three-week wait. The problem urgent care exists to solve disappears.

**Integrated behavioral health.** Mental health in the same building, warm handoffs, no referral maze. The separation of physical and mental health in current systems is a bureaucratic artifact, not a clinical one.

**Community health workers** handle first contact, chronic disease coordination, health education, and the connective tissue between the clinic and members' daily lives. They multiply physician capacity without substituting for it.

## Training and Credentials

Medical training in the US is genuinely extensive for good reasons — the knowledge required to practice medicine safely is deep and the cost of incompetence is serious harm to people. The length and expense of current training is not entirely artificial.

What is artificial:
- The 4-year undergraduate prerequisite, which functions as a wealth and compliance filter rather than a knowledge requirement
- Administrative and billing training that consumes significant residency time but has no clinical value
- Licensing and accreditation structures that protect physician supply restrictions as much as they protect patients

What is genuinely necessary:
- The clinical knowledge base — pharmacology, pathology, physiology, differential diagnosis
- Supervised clinical hours under real conditions. Judgment is learned by doing it, not by studying it.
- Procedural skills for anything invasive

The community's relationship to medical training is through the federation. Universities and hospitals function as infrastructure nodes — they provide training facilities, clinical exposure, and credentialing that no community can replicate internally. The community's role is to sponsor members through that training and receive them back as positioned workers.

**The sponsorship model inverts the current incentive structure.** Currently physicians train at enormous personal cost, carry large debt, and flow toward high-paying markets and away from underserved communities. Under the commons model the community sponsors the training as a community investment, the trainee remains a community member throughout, and they return to a position that was waiting for them. No debt, no job market, no incentive to leave for wealthier patients.

The degree still means something — the clinical knowledge it certifies is real. What the community does not need to replicate is the bureaucratic and financial overhead that surrounds it.

## What the Community Cannot Handle

Honest about limits even at scale:

- **Rare disease** — diagnostic complexity requiring subspecialist depth. Refer to federation network.
- **Major surgery** — the community clinic performs routine procedures; complex surgery requires hospital node infrastructure.
- **Oncology** — manageable for common cancers with the right specialist positioned; rare cancers refer out.
- **Advanced mental health** — the clinic handles common presentations well; severe treatment-resistant conditions require specialist input from the network.

The federation exists in part so these limits don't mean abandonment. A member with cancer is not on their own because the community clinic can't treat cancer — they have access to the network's specialist capacity, with the community supporting them through it.

## Emergency Response

EMS belongs in the community's healthcare infrastructure. The core function — assess ill or injured people where they are, stabilize, and transport to the appropriate level of care — is genuinely valuable and not replaceable by a clinic. A physician in a clinic cannot respond to a cardiac arrest in a member's home.

The community's EMS model:

**First responders.** Trained members who can respond quickly to emergencies within the community. BLS (basic life support) certification — CPR, airway management, bleeding control — is achievable with relatively short training and should be widespread in the community, not limited to a dedicated unit. The more members who can stabilize a situation in the first minutes, the better outcomes are.

**EMT-level responders.** A dedicated cadre with fuller emergency training — IV access, medication administration, advanced assessment. These are community positions, compensated like any other domain worker. They respond to calls, assess in place, and make transport decisions.

**Transport.** The community maintains at least one equipped vehicle capable of transporting a member to a hospital node when the situation exceeds what the clinic can handle. The decision to transport is clinical, not financial — no member delays calling for help because they're worried about cost.

EMS training is much shorter than physician training — EMT certification is achievable in a few months, paramedic in 1–2 years — which means the community can develop this capacity without relying on the university/hospital node pipeline. It is a natural fit for members with practical inclinations who want to contribute to healthcare without the full physician training path.

## Relationship to Dependency Care

Healthcare and dependency care overlap significantly for members with chronic illness or disability. The boundary is roughly:

- **Healthcare** manages diagnosis, treatment, medication, and medical monitoring
- **Dependency care** manages daily living assistance and ongoing support

A member with advanced Parkinson's needs both. The two domains need explicit coordination channels — not separate silos that leave the member to navigate between them.

---

## The Fundamental Point

A community healthcare system has one job: when a member is sick, someone who can help them is available. Everything else — the billing, the insurance, the authorization, the administration — is overhead generated by a market system that the community doesn't have. Strip that away and what remains is a well-staffed clinic where people know their patients, see them promptly, and have access to specialists when needed. That is not complicated. It is just different from what currently exists.
