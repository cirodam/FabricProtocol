# 1.1 Economic Background

## Why Communities Are Struggling

The short answer is that the economy is very good at concentrating wealth and very bad at distributing it. The communities this framework is designed for are not the victims of bad luck or poor choices — they are the predictable output of a system working more or less as designed.

### Deindustrialization and the Rust Belt

For most of the twentieth century, a factory job was the foundation of a working-class community. It paid enough to buy a house, raise a family, support local businesses. The factory anchored a whole ecosystem: suppliers, shops, schools, tax rolls.

When manufacturing moved — to lower-wage countries after trade liberalization in the 1990s, and increasingly to automated systems — the factory left but the community didn't. What followed was not just unemployment but a cascade. Local businesses lost customers. Property values fell. The tax base collapsed. Schools and infrastructure deteriorated. Young people left to find work elsewhere, accelerating the decline. What remained was an older, poorer, sicker population inheriting the debt and decay.

This is the rust belt. It is not a regional phenomenon — it is the pattern that follows wherever the primary economic anchor of a community is removed.

### Financialization

Over the same period, the economy shifted away from making things toward making money from money. The financial sector grew from a supporting role — providing capital for productive enterprise — to a dominant one. Corporations came under pressure to maximize short-term returns to shareholders. That meant cutting labor costs, offshoring production, buying back stock. The productivity gains from technology and globalization flowed overwhelmingly to capital owners, not workers.

A community with a factory has something real. A community whose main employer is a subsidiary of a private equity firm has a liability: the moment it becomes more profitable to close the plant than to run it, it closes.

### The Global Race to the Bottom

The jobs that left the rust belt didn't disappear — they moved to places where labor was cheaper and environmental and safety regulations were weaker. This is presented as development, as lifting people out of poverty. Sometimes it is. More often it is a transfer of exploitation: the same model that extracted value from workers in Ohio or Yorkshire relocated to Bangladesh or Honduras, where workers had even less leverage to push back.

The mechanism is straightforward. A corporation threatens to move production unless wages are cut or taxes reduced. Governments — desperate for jobs — comply. The corporation captures the gains. Workers in both countries are left competing against each other in a race to the bottom, each being told that the other is the threat.

This is reinforced by trade agreements written primarily by and for corporations, international debt conditions that force governments to privatize public assets and cut social programs, and financial systems that allow capital to move freely across borders while people cannot. The result is that poor countries remain poor not despite being integrated into the global economy but often because of how they are integrated into it — as sources of cheap labor and raw materials, with the profits flowing elsewhere.

A community in rural Appalachia and a community in rural Guatemala are not each other's enemies. They are both on the losing end of the same arrangement.

### The Debt-Money System

Money in the modern economy is almost entirely created by banks when they make loans. This means the money supply is tied to debt — and debt requires growth to service it. A community that isn't growing, isn't borrowing, isn't "productive" in the way the system measures productivity, gets starved of the medium of exchange it needs to function. Not because it lacks resources or willing workers, but because it lacks access to money.

This is one reason community currencies and mutual credit systems emerge in crises: not as an ideological statement, but as a practical response to the fact that official money has stopped flowing.

### What the Market Doesn't Do

Markets allocate resources based on purchasing power, not need. A community where people are poor but have genuine needs — for food, shelter, healthcare, care work — looks like a market failure. The people are there. The needs are there. The labor to meet those needs is there. What's missing is the medium of exchange that would let supply meet demand.

The market will not fix this on its own. Capital flows toward returns, not toward need. The formal economy has no mechanism for recognizing the inherent value of a community's existence.

That is the gap this framework is designed to fill.
