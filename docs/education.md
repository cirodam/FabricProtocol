# Education

## Purpose

The education domain exists to produce capable, engaged community members — not to sort people for a labor market. In a community whose monetary system guarantees basic needs regardless of employment, the entire premise of schooling shifts. Children are not being prepared to compete for jobs. They are being prepared to live well, contribute meaningfully when the community needs them, and participate fully in self-governance.

This distinction changes everything about how education is designed.

---

## What's Wrong With the Current Model

American schooling was designed in the 19th century to produce compliant factory workers and soldiers. The Prussian model — age-segregated cohorts, teachers at the front, bells controlling movement, standardized tests measuring conformity — was a deliberate industrial design, not a discovery about how humans learn best. It persists because it serves institutions that benefit from compliant, credential-seeking workers.

The downstream effects are well documented:

- **Compliance over capability.** Thirteen years of being told what to learn, when to learn it, and exactly how to demonstrate it produces adults who are uncomfortable with self-direction and novelty.
- **Credentialism over learning.** The degree became the product. Students optimize for grades rather than understanding. The credential signals employability; the actual knowledge is secondary.
- **Age-segregation is unnatural.** Mixed-age learning is how humans transmitted knowledge for most of history. Older children teaching younger ones benefits both. Industrial schooling abolished this and replaced it with artificial uniformity.
- **Learned helplessness.** Children who spend a decade being managed develop a deep dependence on external direction. This is the opposite of what a self-governing community needs.
- **Severed from real life.** School is a holding environment separate from where adults actually do things. Children watch simulations of productive activity rather than participating in it. The transition to adult contribution is then needlessly difficult.

The good news is that most of these pathologies are downstream of the false premise that school exists to prepare children for employment. Remove that premise and the design naturally improves.

## Structure

Education is organized in three phases, not by rigid age cutoffs but by developmental readiness.

### Phase 1 — Foundational (roughly ages 5–12)

Structured instruction in literacy, numeracy, and basic science. Small groups with dedicated teachers. This phase is genuinely valuable and worth preserving from the conventional model — the skills it builds are real prerequisites for everything else.

The difference from conventional schooling:
- No standardized testing or grades. The community knows its children; a score on a worksheet adds nothing.
- Mixed-age groupings within the phase where appropriate. A nine-year-old helping a six-year-old with reading is learning as much as the six-year-old.
- Teachers are well compensated workers, not underpaid labor absorbing the cost of an underfunded public good.

### Phase 2 — Applied (roughly ages 12–16)

Apprenticeships within functional community domains. The student works alongside real domain workers doing real work — in the food domain, the healthcare domain, construction, energy, whatever the student and community agree on. This is not a simulation. The student contributes actual labor at reduced capacity while acquiring genuine skills.

Both sides benefit. The domain gets capable additional hands. The student learns by doing, under real conditions, with real stakes. The relationship between student and mentor is the oldest educational relationship there is.

Apprenticeships create intentional dependencies between the education domain and other domains. A student placed in the food domain is partially the food domain's responsibility during that placement. This should be made explicit in the governance structure.

### Phase 3 — Self-Directed (roughly 16+)

Philosophy, science, advanced domain specialization, art, mathematics, engineering — whatever the member chooses to pursue. The community provides access to knowledge and knowledgeable members. Direction belongs to the individual.

This is not the education domain's mandate in the operational sense. The domain provides the foundation and the apprenticeship infrastructure. What a member does with that foundation is their own.

## Teachers

Teachers are among the most important workers in the community. The knowledge and values they transmit shape the next generation's capacity to sustain and improve everything the community has built.

This means teachers must be:

**Well compensated.** Teacher wages in the current system are chronically insufficient relative to the skill, responsibility, and social value of the work. This is not an accident — it reflects a society that claims to value education while systematically underfunding it. A commons community cannot replicate this. Teacher positions must be compensated at least as well as any other skilled domain work, and likely better given the long-term leverage of the role.

**Respected.** Compensation is necessary but not sufficient. Teachers should hold recognized status in the community's governance structures. Their professional judgment about pedagogy should be trusted. They should not be subordinated to administrative compliance requirements that prioritize metrics over learning.

**Supported.** Teaching is demanding work. Teachers need time for preparation, for their own continued learning, for peer consultation with other teachers. A teacher who is burned out or intellectually stagnant cannot transmit curiosity and capability. The community's investment in teachers' ongoing development is an investment in everyone the teachers will ever teach.

The commons community that treats its teachers as essential infrastructure — rather than as a cost to be minimized — is the one whose children grow up capable of sustaining it.

---

## Relationship to the Three-Layer Income Model

Every community member — including children and students — receives the food voucher floor allocation unconditionally. No one starves because they are learning rather than producing.

Students in the applied phase who contribute real labor to domain work are compensated accordingly, at rates appropriate to their contribution level. This is not charity or a token gesture — it is honest recognition that their labor has value.

A young person who completes the foundational and applied phases and chooses not to hold a community position still receives the food voucher floor and the commons dividend. The community does not require continuous employment for survival. It does invite contribution, and most members will choose to contribute because the community's needs are visible and the social value of contribution is real.

## Who Should Teach

The honest credential for a community teacher is: do the children actually learn from this person, and do the other members trust them with that responsibility? The community can assess both without a state license.

Current credentialing systems have some genuine content buried under significant bureaucratic noise. What's worth keeping:

**Genuinely valuable regardless of credential:**
- Deep subject knowledge. A person who actually understands mathematics, ecology, or history can teach it. The degree that certified this is irrelevant; the knowledge is real and demonstrable.
- Experience working with children. Knowing how children at different ages actually think, what confuses them, how to explain the same thing four different ways. This comes from doing it, not from coursework.
- Structured literacy methods. There is real science about what works for early reading instruction. Foundational phase teachers should know it.
- Basic safeguarding. Recognizing signs of abuse and knowing how to respond. Essential regardless of state involvement.

**Credential requirements that are mostly noise:**
- State teaching licenses. These exist to regulate a labor market and allow states to control curriculum. They have no pedagogical content.
- Education degrees from schools of education. Widely regarded even within academia as thin on substance. A deep knowledge of the subject being taught is worth more.

**The most important point: experienced members make the best teachers.**

A 60-year-old who spent 30 years farming, building, or practicing medicine has knowledge that no recent graduate can replicate. The community's most valuable teachers are often its most experienced members — people whose knowledge is proven by decades of real application under real conditions. The Foundational phase needs people who are skilled at teaching children. The Applied phase needs people who know their domain deeply. These are different skills and the community likely has both, concentrated in its older members.

This also means older members have a natural role that keeps them engaged and valued as their physical capacity changes. Teaching is work the community needs, it draws on accumulated knowledge, and it is not physically demanding. A governance structure that recognizes this — that creates funded teaching positions accessible to experienced elders — builds something conventional schooling has entirely lost: the transmission of hard-won knowledge from the people who actually hold it.

---

## The Deeper Point

Every pathology of American education is downstream of the premise that school exists to sort people for a labor market. Teach to tests because employers need credentials. Enforce compliance because factories need workers who follow instructions. Sever children from real community life because their time belongs to the sorting process, not to them.

Remove that premise entirely. The community's job is not to produce labor market inputs. It is to produce people who can live well together and sustain what they've built. That is a completely different design problem, and it has a much better answer.
