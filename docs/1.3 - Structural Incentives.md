# 1.3 Structural Incentives

The standard explanation for why things are bad is that bad people made bad decisions. Corporations are greedy. Politicians are corrupt. Consumers are lazy or selfish. If only those people would change, things would improve.

This explanation is wrong — or rather, it is incomplete to the point of being useless. It locates the problem inside individuals when the problem is in the architecture of the system they inhabit. Individual actors, in most cases, are behaving rationally. The system is irrational.

Understanding this distinction is not an excuse for anyone's behavior. It is a precondition for doing anything about it.

---

## What Structural Incentives Are

A structural incentive is a feature of a system that makes a particular behavior rational — not necessarily moral, not necessarily good, just rational in the narrow sense that it benefits the actor who pursues it.

When a system has bad structural incentives, it reliably produces bad outcomes regardless of who is operating within it. You can replace the people and the behavior persists, because the behavior is what the system rewards.

The current economy has several of these, and they interact.

---

## Externalities

The most basic broken incentive is the externality. An externality is a cost — or benefit — that falls on someone who didn't participate in the transaction that created it.

A factory that discharges chemicals into a river is producing goods at a lower cost than a factory that treats its waste. The savings are real. The pollution is also real, but it is paid by people downstream — not by the factory, not by the customers who bought the cheaper product. The cost has been externalized.

From the factory's perspective this is rational. From the perspective of the people downstream it is theft. From the perspective of the system as a whole it is catastrophic, because the price signal — the mechanism that is supposed to communicate real costs — is broken. The product's price does not reflect what it actually costs to make it.

This is not a market failure in the sense of an exception. It is how markets work when there are no limits on cost externalization. The incentive is to externalize as much as possible, because whoever externalizes the least is at a competitive disadvantage.

---

## The Race to the Bottom

Externalization interacts with competition to produce what is sometimes called the race to the bottom.

If one employer can pay lower wages by keeping workers precarious and replacing them when they wear out, that employer can undercut competitors on price. Competitors face a choice: match those conditions or lose market share. The rational move is to follow. The equilibrium, absent external constraints, is the minimum that workers will accept given their alternatives — and employers have every incentive to reduce those alternatives.

The same logic applies to environmental standards, safety regulations, and labor protections. Capital moves toward the lowest floor. Jurisdictions that maintain higher floors lose investment. Jurisdictions that want investment lower their floors. The equilibrium is the bottom.

This is not a conspiracy. Each decision in the chain is individually rational. The irrationality is systemic: the outcome no one would choose — a world with degraded labor, degraded environments, and degraded communities — emerges from a series of individually sensible choices.

---

## Short-Termism

Financial markets reward performance measured in quarters. A company whose stock trades publicly is under constant pressure to show improving returns on a short horizon. The rational manager maximizes for that horizon.

What gets sacrificed is everything on a longer timescale: maintenance of infrastructure, investment in worker development, sustainable use of natural resources, long-term relationships with suppliers and communities. These investments have real value, but they don't show up on this quarter's earnings. They may not show up for a decade. The incentive is to harvest them.

This is not a failure of imagination. Managers understand that these investments matter. The system simply does not reward them for making those investments, and punishes them if they do while competitors don't. So they don't.

The tragedy is that short-termism is self-undermining. It destroys the capacity it depends on. But by the time that destruction becomes visible, the people who made the decisions that caused it are no longer in the positions they held. Accountability is structurally deferred.

---

## The Growth Imperative

Most money in the modern economy is created as debt. When a bank makes a loan, the principal is deposited into existence. But the interest is not. It was never created. It has to come from somewhere — which means it has to come from new loans. The money supply must grow continuously just to service the debt that already exists.

This is not a design flaw that was introduced accidentally. It is what the system is. The consequence is that an economy that stops growing doesn't just stagnate — it begins to collapse. Debt cannot be serviced. Defaults cascade. Jobs disappear. The political and social pressure to restart growth becomes overwhelming.

So growth is not a goal anyone chose. It is a structural requirement. And structural requirements shape behavior.

A firm that is not growing is losing ground to competitors who are. A market that is not expanding is one where firms fight over fixed share, which means lower margins, which means pressure to cut costs everywhere. An economy that is not growing is one where debts go bad, which means banks tighten, which means less investment, which means less employment, which means less consumption — the self-reinforcing spiral in the other direction.

Every actor in this system is incentivized to grow. Not because growth is inherently desirable, but because the alternative is deterioration. Growth is the water the system swims in, and not swimming means sinking.

The consequences are considerable. Growth requires inputs: energy, materials, land, labor. A system that must grow perpetually must consume perpetually. There is no stable state, no equilibrium point at which the consumption of resources stops increasing. Sustainability is structurally off the menu — not because anyone opposes it, but because the system cannot function without growth, and growth cannot happen without consuming more.

It also means that any activity can be justified if it contributes to growth. Producing things no one needs. Degrading things that were working. Manufacturing problems to sell solutions. The market does not distinguish between growth that increases wellbeing and growth that merely increases transaction volume. A community that gets sick and requires medical care contributes to GDP. A community that stays healthy and cares for its own does not.

The growth imperative is not compatible with a stable planet or a stable community. It produces the behaviors it needs to sustain itself, and those behaviors are destructive. Not because anyone decided to be destructive. Because the system requires it.

---

## Concentration and Capture

Competition produces winners. Winners accumulate advantages. Advantages can be used to win more.

A large enough firm can lobby against competition, buy competitors before they mature, control the regulatory environment it operates in, and extract rents from the infrastructure others depend on. A large enough financial institution can be too big to fail — meaning its losses are socialized and its gains remain private. A large enough platform can become the default and then charge whatever the market will bear.

This is not corruption in the crude sense, though corruption is part of it. It is the rational use of scale and position. The system rewards it. Power, once accumulated, uses itself to accumulate more power. The endpoint is not a competitive market — it is a monopoly or oligopoly that uses the language of markets to defend its position while systematically preventing the competition it claims to embody.

---

## Precarity as a Tool

Workers who have savings, alternatives, and community support can refuse bad conditions. Workers who are one missed paycheck from catastrophe cannot.

The level of precarity in the existing economy is not accidental. It is the predictable output of a system in which employers benefit from precarity and have the power to maintain it. High consumer debt, inadequate social insurance, housing costs that consume most of a paycheck, the erosion of unions — each of these reduces the worker's alternatives and increases their tolerance for bad conditions. Each is rational from the employer's perspective. Each is harmful to the worker and, at sufficient scale, to the community.

---

## The Pattern

These incentives share a structure. In each case:

- An actor can capture a benefit while pushing the cost onto someone else
- The system provides no mechanism to prevent this, and may reward it
- Competitors face pressure to do the same
- The aggregate outcome — degraded workers, degraded environments, degraded communities — is one nobody would choose, emerging from choices everyone is making

The individuals inside this system are not, in most cases, monsters. They are responding to the signals the system sends. The system is sending the wrong signals.

---

## Inverting the Incentives

The question is whether you can build a system that sends different signals — one where the rational thing to do is also the healthy thing to do.

A community currency system can do this, not perfectly, but structurally, in ways that compound.

**The economy is closed.** When production and consumption happen within the same community, costs cannot be easily externalized. The pollution enters your water. The worker you underpay is your neighbor. The community you extract from is the community you live in. You bear the consequences of the system you participate in creating.

**Demurrage replaces hoarding.** Holding currency loses value over time; circulating it does not. The rational move is to spend, invest, or pay forward — not to accumulate. Wealth held passively works against the holder. Wealth put into motion serves the community and returns.

**Currency represents persons.** Because the unit of account is anchored to human presence, the interests of the currency and the interests of people are structurally aligned. An economy that impoverishes its members is destroying its own monetary base. Keeping people healthy and present is not charity — it is the foundation of the money supply.

**The commons are funded automatically.** Demurrage flows to the commons. Every unit of currency in circulation is continuously contributing to shared infrastructure, simply by existing. There is no moment at which the commons must compete with private interests for funding — the funding mechanism is built into the currency itself.

**Governance is participatory.** Community members govern the community. They do not vote to poison their own water supply. They do not vote to make themselves precarious. The interests of the governed and the interests of the system are, again structurally, aligned — not perfectly, because communities contain conflicts, but they cannot externalize those conflicts onto people who have no voice.

None of this requires that everyone become virtuous. The point is that rational actors in this system are pushed toward behaviors that sustain the community rather than behaviors that extract from it. The architecture does the work that character is now expected to do, and failing to do.

---

## What This Framework Is Doing

Every design decision in this framework is oriented toward these structural alignments.

The currency is issued by the community and anchored to persons, so extraction cannot happen without harming the currency itself. Demurrage funds the commons, so maintenance is automatic. Governance is participatory, so the community can see what is happening and respond. The economy is local enough that consequences are visible and shared.

This does not guarantee good outcomes. Communities can make mistakes, can be captured by internal factions, can fail to respond to visible harms. But the system is not working against them. The incentives run in the right direction.

That is the difference between repairing individuals and repairing architecture. Architecture can be changed. Once changed, it shapes behavior at scale, without requiring everyone to be better than they are.
