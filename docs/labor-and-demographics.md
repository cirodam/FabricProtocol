# Community Labor and Demographics

## Overview

Sustainable community design requires understanding how many people are available to work,
what each domain needs from that labor pool, and how mechanization changes the calculus.
These numbers are estimates based on historical and contemporary cooperative communities.
Real communities will vary by geography, climate, age distribution, and available technology.

---

## Baseline Demographics

For a stable, self-reproducing community the population distributes roughly as follows:

| Group | Age | Share | Per 1,000 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Young children | 0–5 | ~8% | 80 |
| School age | 6–17 | ~14% | 140 |
| Young adults | 18–29 | ~16% | 160 |
| Core productive | 30–54 | ~30% | 300 |
| Late productive | 55–64 | ~12% | 120 |
| Early elderly | 65–74 | ~11% | 110 |
| Late elderly | 75+ | ~9% | 90 |

Working-age adults (18–64): approximately **580 per 1,000 members**.

### Subtract from available labor pool:
- Primary caregivers for young children: ~60
- Members with significant disability: ~40
- Members in full-time training or transition: ~20

**Available FTEs: roughly 460 per 1,000 members.**

## The Effect of Mechanization on Agricultural Labor

Agriculture is the load-bearing constraint. It dominates labor allocation more than any
other domain, and mechanization is the single highest-leverage improvement available.

| Method | FTEs per 1,000 people fed |
|---|---|
| Hand tools only | 150–200 |
| Animal draft power | 80–120 |
| Tractors + basic mechanization | 30–50 |
| Modern industrial (with external inputs) | 2–5 |

Pre-industrial societies spent 70–80% of their labor on food. Industrial agriculture
brought that to 2% — but with deep fossil fuel and supply chain dependencies. A resilient
community using tractors, basic implements, and locally produced biodiesel lands at
**6–10% of available labor** — the most practical target.

### Why mechanization is realistic:

- Tractors are durable and repairable with widely distributed knowledge
- Older designs (1950s–1970s) are particularly maintainable — parts are simple and
  most can be fabricated in a community machine shop
- A community of 1,000–5,000 needs 2–3 tractors with a shared implement pool, not a fleet
- Diesel can be produced from waste vegetable oil or dedicated biodiesel crops,
  reducing dependence on external fuel supply chains

## County-Scale Analysis (15,000 People)

Based on typical rural American county demographics:

### Age Distribution

| Group | Age | % | Count |
|-------|-----|---|-------|
| Children | 0–17 | 22% | 3,300 |
| Young adult | 18–24 | 9% | 1,350 |
| Prime working | 25–54 | 37% | 5,550 |
| Late career | 55–64 | 13% | 1,950 |
| Early retirement | 65–74 | 11% | 1,650 |
| Elderly | 75+ | 8% | 1,200 |

Working-age population (18–64): **~8,850 people**

### Productive Capacity Within Working Age

| Condition | % of working-age | Count |
|-----------|-----------------|-------|
| Fully capable | 60% | 5,310 |
| Partially capable (chronic illness, disability, caregiver role) | 25% | 2,210 |
| Unable to work (severe disability, serious illness) | 15% | 1,330 |

**Effective labor pool: ~5,300–6,500 people**

A planning figure of **4,000 committed workers** is conservative and appropriate — it accounts
for people who choose not to take community employment, are in education, or are transitioning
between roles.

### Dependency Ratio

| Group | Count |
|-------|-------|
| Children (0–17) | 3,300 |
| Elderly (65+) | 2,850 |
| Unable to work | 1,330 |
| **Total dependents** | **~7,480** |

```
dependency ratio = dependents / workers = 7,480 / 4,000 ≈ 1.9
```

Each worker sustains themselves plus roughly 1.9 dependents. Modern industrial economies
sustain ratios of 1.7 or worse — this is manageable. The full potential labor pool (5,300+)
provides headroom to scale up services or reduce working hours without needing population growth.

### Essential Workforce Requirements

Out of 4,000 committed workers, roughly 28% need to be in essential services:

| Domain | Workers needed | % of workforce |
|--------|---------------|----------------|
| Food production & distribution | 200 | 5% |
| Healthcare | 160 | 4% |
| Education | 200 | 5% |
| Water & sanitation | 80 | 2% |
| Energy | 80 | 2% |
| Transport & logistics | 120 | 3% |
| Governance & administration | 80 | 2% |
| Construction & maintenance | 200 | 5% |
| **Total essential** | **~1,120** | **~28%** |

This leaves **~2,880 workers** for non-essential commerce — the market economy that gives
credits real purchasing power. Without a non-essential sector, allowances are credits chasing
nothing, which inflates prices and erodes M in real terms.

## Why Scale Matters

The fiscal equation (see [demurrage-sustainability.md](demurrage-sustainability.md)) is
scale-invariant — a 500-person community can balance the rate/M math with large enough
endowments. But the labor constraint is not:

- A 500-person community cannot staff a functional hospital
- It cannot run full food production at scale
- It likely lacks the skilled workers to cover all essential domains simultaneously

The ~15,000 threshold is not about reaching a target M in nominal terms. It is about having
enough people to **staff all essential services while maintaining a non-essential sector large
enough to make credits worth holding**.

County-scale (10,000–20,000) is the natural unit for this model because it is the smallest
population that can plausibly close both the labor and fiscal constraints simultaneously.
  breaking the external fuel dependency over time

A functional machine shop that can maintain agricultural equipment is therefore one of
the highest-leverage investments in the entire community — it multiplies the productive
output of every other investment in the food and agriculture domains.

## Scale Effects

The minimum viable size for genuine self-sufficiency with domain specialization is
approximately **3,000–5,000 members**. Below this threshold, members must cover multiple
domains simultaneously — the nurse who also farms, the teacher who also does construction.
That works, but resilience depends on a few individuals who cannot be sick or absent.

| Community size | Available FTEs | Agriculture (mechanized) | Remaining |
|---|---|---|---|
| 500 | 230 | 25–35 | 195–205 |
| 1,000 | 460 | 40–60 | 400–420 |
| 3,000 | 1,380 | 120–180 | 1,200–1,260 |
| 5,000 | 2,300 | 200–300 | 2,000–2,100 |
| 15,000 | 6,900 | 600–900 | 6,000–6,300 |
| 30,000 | 13,800 | 1,200–1,800 | 12,000–12,600 |

At **15,000–30,000 members**, the community achieves genuine depth in every domain:
multiple clinicians, specialist farmers, dedicated engineers, teachers who only teach.
At this scale the community stops being fragile and becomes robust — capable of absorbing
the loss of key individuals without domain failure.

## The Flexible Reserve

The labor surplus above required FTEs is not waste — it is resilience. It serves:

- **Seasonal agriculture peaks** — harvest and planting require temporary labor surges
- **Construction projects** — capital projects draw on the reserve without defunding domains
- **Emergency response** — flood, fire, and health crises require surge capacity
- **Member transitions** — illness, caregiving, training, and onboarding all take people
  temporarily out of their primary roles
- **Innovation and improvement** — members with slack time experiment, improve processes,
  and develop the next generation of community capacity

A community with no reserve is operating at maximum extraction. The first disruption
overwhelms it. A community with 15–20% reserve capacity is genuinely resilient.

---

## Key Ratios to Monitor

These ratios give a quick read on community labor health:

- **Dependency ratio** — (children + elderly) / working adults. Target: below 0.8.
- **Agricultural labor share** — agriculture FTEs / total available FTEs. Target: 10–15% mechanized.
- **Care labor share** — (child care + dependency care + healthcare) FTEs / total. Target: 12–18%.
- **Reserve ratio** — unused FTEs / total available. Target: 15–20% minimum.

If agricultural labor share rises above 20%, the community is under-mechanized or
over-extended on acreage. If reserve ratio falls below 10%, the community is brittle
and a single domain crisis becomes a multi-domain crisis.
