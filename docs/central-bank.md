# The Central Bank

## What it is

The CentralBank is the sole issuer of money in the community. It has one job: put money into the hands of members so they can participate in the economy, and manage that money supply honestly over time.

It is not a bank in the conventional sense. It does not take deposits, charge interest, or serve as an intermediary between savers and borrowers. It is a monetary authority governed by and accountable to the community.

---

## The membership endowment

When a member joins, the CentralBank issues them an **endowment** — a grant of purchasing power that bootstraps their participation. This is not a loan. Members are not expected to repay it. It is the community saying: *you are a member, and membership means you can participate*.

The endowment can be topped up over time as a member's trust score grows.

When a member leaves or dies, their remaining balance (up to the endowment amount) is returned to the bank. Any surplus they accumulated through their own labor stays in the community — transferred to the Commons. Any shortfall (if they spent down their balance before leaving) is left as a negative bank account, recovered gradually through demurrage.

---

## Trust and endowment access

A new member's endowment is limited. Trust is earned over time — through presence, participation, and longevity in the community. As a member's trust score grows, so does their access to endowment.

This is not a gatekeeping mechanism. It is a recognition that trust is built, not assumed. A community cannot know a new member the way it knows someone who has shown up for years. Extending full monetary access immediately would be naive; withholding it permanently would be exclusionary. The trust score is the middle path.

The philosophy is simple: the longer you are part of a community, the more the community can extend to you — and the more you can extend to it.

This also means the community has a stake in every member's flourishing. When someone joins, the money supply grows and everyone gains a new participant and contributor. When someone lives a long, active life in the community, their sustained presence and rising trust benefit the whole. Growth in membership and longevity are not just social goods — they are monetary ones.

---

## How money is created

Money is created from nothing when an endowment is issued. The bank's account goes negative; the member's account goes positive. The bank's negative balance *is* the money supply. This is not a problem — it is the system working correctly.

Crucially, the CentralBank is the only entity in the system that ever carries a negative balance. Members cannot go into debt. Domains cannot be overdrawn. The Commons cannot borrow against future production. Every account in the community is either at zero or positive — the only obligation in the system is the bank's, and that obligation is to the community itself.

In conventional banking, this is how money works too. The difference is that here, the community controls the issuance. There is no profit motive, no interest accrual, and no external creditor.

The money supply grows when members join and shrinks when they leave. This is by design. The currency is a measure of community participation, not an abstract store of value decoupled from the people it serves. A larger community needs more money in circulation; a smaller one needs less. The supply adjusts automatically, without policy decisions or external intervention.

---

## Deflation

As the community's productive capacity grows — more food produced, more housing maintained, more services available — the same amount of money buys more. This is deflation, and here it is not a problem. It is a sign that the community is working.

Conventional economics treats deflation as dangerous because debt-based economies require growth to service their obligations. If prices fall, debtors can't repay; the system seizes. But this community carries no such debt. There are no creditors demanding returns. A falling price level simply means members' money goes further — a direct transfer of the gains from collective productivity back to every member who holds it.

Deflation here is the economy doing what it is supposed to do: making life more affordable as the community gets better at meeting its own needs.

---

## Demurrage

Demurrage is a periodic fee assessed on all non-exempt account balances. It is the mechanism by which the community discourages hoarding and keeps money circulating.

Practically, demurrage also serves a monetary function: it gradually contracts the money supply after member exits result in shortfalls. If a departed member spent their entire endowment before leaving, that money is still in circulation — held by other members. Demurrage slowly draws it back to the bank, restoring balance over time.

Demurrage only runs when money is actually in circulation (i.e. the bank's account is negative). If the supply is already balanced, there is nothing to recover.

### Why demurrage works

Conventional money rewards holding. A dollar saved is a dollar available for investment — and investment earns more dollars. The incentive is to accumulate.

Money with demurrage rewards circulation. Holding it costs you. Spending it, lending it, investing it in community production — all of those are preferable to watching it decay. The incentive is to keep value moving through the community rather than pooling at the top.

---

## What this makes possible

- **Equal entry.** Every member starts with real purchasing power, not just theoretical membership.
- **No debt at the door.** Joining the community is not a financial obligation.
- **Honest money supply.** The community can see exactly how much money exists and why.
- **Self-correcting.** Demurrage and endowment reclaim mean the system tends toward balance without requiring active intervention.
- **Community-controlled monetary policy.** The community votes on endowment levels. There is no external authority setting the terms.

---

## What this is not

The CentralBank does not set interest rates, manage inflation targets, or serve as a lender of last resort in the conventional sense. Those are tools designed for a different kind of economy — one that runs on debt, speculation, and capital accumulation.

This system runs on contribution and need. The monetary tools reflect that.
