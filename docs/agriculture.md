# Agriculture

## Framing

A community forming in rural America is not building agriculture from scratch. Rural counties already have working farms, tractors, grain elevators, co-ops, and farmers with decades of soil knowledge. The Agriculture domain is not an agrarian village being constructed — it is an organizational layer that helps the community coordinate around existing agricultural capacity.

The key questions are not "how do we grow food?" but:
- Which farmers are community members, and how is their labor compensated?
- What land is collectively owned or leased?
- What equipment is a community asset versus personally owned?
- What new infrastructure does the community need to build?

## Functional units

### FarmOperation

A working farm — previously family-owned, leased, or newly established — operated by community members. The community may have several FarmOperations with different acreage, crop specializations, and equipment. Tractors, implements, barns, and silos are assets on the FarmOperation's ledger. Farmers are community members holding positions here and compensated through the normal payroll system.

A community with tractors and 300–500 acres needs approximately **8–12 field operators** total across all FarmOperations. This is dramatically lower than hand-labor estimates — a tractor operator can work 50–100 acres.

### Greenhouse

Purpose-built community infrastructure for season extension and seedling production. Unlike FarmOperations, a greenhouse is unlikely to exist already and is typically the Agriculture domain's first significant capital project.

Primary functions:
- Seedling propagation for all field operations (adds 6–8 weeks to effective growing season)
- Year-round production of greens, herbs, and high-nutrition crops
- Experimental and heirloom variety grow-outs for the SeedLibrary
- Crop trials before committing field acreage

Staffing: **3–5 FTEs**

### SeedLibrary

Active management of the community's seed supply and genetic diversity. Seed sovereignty is a prerequisite for food sovereignty — hybrid and GMO seeds cannot be saved; even open-pollinated seeds require regular grow-outs (every 3–5 years) to maintain viability and local adaptation.

The SeedLibrary is an institutional knowledge anchor. The people who run it carry understanding of which varieties perform in this specific place, climate, and soil — knowledge that cannot be looked up.

Staffing: **1–2 FTEs** (plus seasonal grow-out labor)

### CompostingYard

Closes the nutrient loop between organic waste and agricultural soil. Accepts inputs from Sanitation (organic waste), Food (kitchen and cannery scraps, spoilage), and FarmOperations (crop residue, manure). Returns finished compost to FarmOperations and the Greenhouse as the primary soil amendment.

Without this loop, the community continuously depletes soil fertility while purchasing synthetic replacements. Farms dependent on external fertility inputs are one supply chain disruption away from soil that cannot support a crop.

Staffing: **2–3 FTEs**

## Staffing summary (per 1,000 members, with tractors)

| Unit | Staff (FTE) |
|---|---|
| FarmOperation(s) | 8–12 |
| Greenhouse | 3–5 |
| SeedLibrary | 1–2 |
| CompostingYard | 2–3 |

**Total: ~14–22 FTEs (~1.5–2.5% of population)**

Combined with the food processing chain (~28 FTEs), the full food system requires approximately **42–50 FTEs**, or about **4–5% of a 1,000-person community**.

## What this doesn't cover

- **Water** — irrigation for hundreds of acres in a dry year is a serious infrastructure problem; not modeled
- **Livestock** — animals integrated into farm operations (manure, draft, protein) are assets within FarmOperations, not separate units
- **Fuel and energy** — tractor-based agriculture requires diesel or electricity; the source of that energy is a separate planning concern
- **Procurement** — if the community contracts with outside farms rather than operating its own, that is a procurement relationship, not a functional unit
