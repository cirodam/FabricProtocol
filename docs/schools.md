# Schools as Community Infrastructure

## The Overlooked Asset

Every American neighborhood already contains a building designed to feed hundreds of people, shelter large crowds, host assemblies, and serve children in groups — and it is publicly owned. Schools are the most underutilized piece of community infrastructure in the country.

During normal times they sit empty for three months of the year and dark after 3pm. During a crisis they become the obvious gathering point, because everyone already knows where they are.

The Commons model should treat the nearest school not as a building to work around, but as a potential nucleus.

---

## What Schools Provide

**Assembly space**
Gyms and auditoriums can hold hundreds of people for meetings, coordination, or emergency shelter. This is harder to replicate than it looks — most buildings simply do not have a room that fits a whole neighborhood.

**Convertible rooms**
Classrooms become dormitories, medical triage rooms, childcare spaces, functional domain offices, or meeting rooms with minimal effort. The room count, sizing, and division into wings maps almost directly onto a Commons hub layout.

**Commercial kitchens**
School cafeteria kitchens are designed to produce hundreds of meals a day. They have commercial refrigeration, industrial ranges, ventilation, and food storage. This infrastructure would take significant capital to build from scratch.

**Bathrooms at crowd scale**
Facilities are sized for hundreds of users. Schools with gyms often have locker rooms with showers — adequate for an overnight population with modest retrofitting.

**Backup power**
Many schools carry backup generators specifically because they are designated emergency shelters under FEMA and state emergency management plans. The emergency function is already legally recognized.

**Geographic distribution**
Schools were sited to be within walking distance of the residential neighborhoods they serve. The coverage pattern is already optimized for the same problem a Commons network tries to solve: every member within reach of a hub on foot.

**Public ownership**
No acquisition cost. No buyout. No landlord. The building already belongs to the community in the most literal sense.

## The Crisis-to-Commons Sequence

Schools follow a predictable arc when a crisis is severe enough:

1. **Shelter activation** — people arrive at the nearest school because it is known, open, and large enough
2. **Informal coordination** — someone starts organizing food, someone else handles medical, a third person is tracking who has arrived
3. **Role formalization** — as the crisis extends past a day or two, informal roles become explicit: cooks, caregivers, a logistics coordinator, someone managing the supply inventory
4. **Functional domains emerge** — the kitchen becomes the Food domain, the nurse's office becomes Healthcare, the gym becomes Commons hall
5. **Prolonged crisis becomes permanent community** — if displacement is long enough, the dormitory arrangement stabilizes, new members arrive, the school *is* the Commons

This is not hypothetical. It is the documented arc of disaster communities, refugee settlements, and mutual aid networks under stress. The school provides the physical container that makes the transition faster and less chaotic.

---

## Limitations

Schools are designed for children during the day, not residential density or 24/7 operation.

- **Kitchens** can produce volume but are not designed for continuous residential cooking by rotating crews of adults
- **Shower capacity** is often inadequate for overnight populations outside of facilities with full locker rooms
- **HVAC** is often zoned for daytime occupancy and expensive to run continuously
- **Privacy** is minimal — classrooms have windows, dormitory arrangements are communal by necessity
- **Ownership friction** — a school board or municipality controls the building; in a non-crisis context, using it as a permanent Commons hub requires negotiation or acquisition

These are solvable problems. The point is not that a school is a finished Commons hub, but that it is the best available starting point in most American neighborhoods. A community that can organize inside a school has bought itself the time to build or acquire something better.

---

## Relationship to the Commons Model

The school-as-nucleus model is an **on-ramp**, not a destination. A mature Commons would purpose-build or heavily retrofit its hub to support:

- Continuous residential cooking and dining
- Adequate private sleeping quarters
- Purpose-designed clinic space
- Workshop and fabrication facilities
- Energy generation and storage on-site

But a community that cannot build from scratch — which is most communities — should identify its nearest school, understand its layout and capacity, and treat it as the default assembly point and coordination hub in any disruption scenario.

The transition from "emergency shelter at Lincoln Elementary" to "Commons hub in the Lincoln neighborhood" is a matter of time, organization, and intention. The building is already there.
